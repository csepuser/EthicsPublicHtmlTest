Boolean Searching

Boolean searching is a tool that allows users to define their searches in a more exact way by constructing logical relationships among search terms. While not needed for simple searches, Boolean searching can help eliminate unwanted search results and  even rank search results to favor one term over another.  Unlike some databases which use “AND” “OR” and “NOT,” the NanoEthics Bank (NEB) uses “+,” “-,” and spaces to allow users to create search equations.

To switch from normal to Boolean searching, find the drop down box right below the search box and switch the mode to Boolean.

Here is a list of some of the ways you can formulate your search:

“Or” searches

To search for material that includes any of a number of words, enter the words with only a space between them. This search is helpful when you are not sure which term might be used in an article, such as the words “college” and “university.” 

The search equation  would look like this:

‘college university’ 

and would bring up all records containing either the word college, the word university or  records containing both words (represented by the area where the two circles overlap).

 

“And” searches

To search for material that  must include all specified words, place a plus sign in front of each word. This will make sure all of your search results include all the terms listed.  A search for records about poverty and crime would be written like this

 ‘+poverty  +crime’

And your search results would include records that only include the terms poverity AND crime.

 

 

“Not” searches

If you would like to make sure your search brings up material that does not include a specific term, use a minus symbol.  This will give you results that include one term but not the other.  So a search for material about cats but not about dogs would look like this:

  +Cats –Dogs

and only records that include the word cat, and not the word dog are returned.

 

Ranking importance of terms

You can also rank the importance of words a search.

‘+poverty crime’

will find records that contain the word “poverty” but ranks the record higher if it also contains the word “crime.”

To reduce the importance of a term, use the “~” sign.  Lets say you are interested in material on nanotechnology development  in China, but less interested in seeing material on Asia in general.  You can phrase your search in this way:

‘+China ~Asia’ 

Exact Match

To search for an exact phrase, enclose it in quotation marks. This might be useful when searching for the title of a specific article, a specific author, or words like “United Nations.”  Note that this searches for the phrase literally, as it was typed.

“Nanoethics and human enhancement”

Truncation

You can use the truncation symbol * to search for variations of a single word or stem. 

‘ethic*’

Will search for ethic, ethics, ethical, ethically etc.

 

Advanced Boolean Searching

You can use a combination of the elements described above in one search equation. To solve the problem of having multiple operators in one search equation, parentheses can be strategically placed around similar (usually synonymous) search terms. Sometimes it might take a few tries to get the perfect search equation.

For example, you want to find material on regulation impacting the environment in the United States or the United Kingdom.

A search of:

‘+environmental +regulation +(United Kingdom United States)’

brings up results that include material on the United Nations (notice that this search specifies United OR Kingdom rather then United AND Kingdom).  You also may be missing results by only looking for the term “environmental.”

To formulate the search, you want all terms related to the word environment so you use the truncation symbol (*), and  you want the terms environment AND regulation present in all of the records. To avoid getting United Nations results, you can look for the exact matches of “United Kingdom” and “United States” by using quotation marks.

‘+(environment* +regulation) +(“United Kingdom” “United States”)’

 

 

Advanced ranking of search terms:

If you are looking for articles about a certian subject, but want to see search results from one set of terms ranked higher then results mentioning a second set of terms you can use greater then > and less then < signs in our search. “>” increases the importance of the term, and “<” decreases its importance.  For example, you want to look for material on standards for manufacturers producing nanomaterials, but are more interested in material on voluntary standards rather then standards developed by governmental agencies

+standards +(>voluntary <governmental)

This search finds records that contain the words “standards” and “voluntary”, or “standards” and “governmental” (in any order), but rank “ voluntary standards ” higher than “governmental standards”.

This covers most of the Boolean search capabilities of the NanoEthicsBank. For information on how you can use Boolean searches on the Internet, see the Boolean Search Guide on Search EngineWatch.com
