1. Introductions
This was the fourth meeting of the Software Engineering Coordinating Committee
(SWECC), a joint activity of the ACM and the IEEE Computer Society (CS). Gerald Engel
attended the first part of the meeting to report on the progress of the Guests at this
meeting included representatives of three of the four SWECC projects: Robert Dupuis.and Pierre Bourque for the SWEBOK project, Don Gotterbarn for the code of ethics
project, and Laurie Werth for the education project. Dennis Frailey volunteered to prepare
the minutes of the meeting.

4. Working Session
4.1 ABET/CSAB integration issues
A brief discussion of this subject ensued, primarily around the question of the status of
software engineering accreditation. It was clear that little information was currently
available and no explicit SWEcc action was apparent at this time.
4.2 Missouri Computer Engineering proposal
The SWEcc has been apprised of software engineering licensing proposals in the states
of Indiana, New Mexico, and Missouri in addition to Texas' current licensing activities.
SWEcc suspects there are others as well. Several such proposals are to be discussed
this Fall at the annual, national meeting ot the professional engineering societies and the
NCEES. Several of these bodies have sought advice and direction from SWEcc and its
parent societies but due to the ACM Council's position on licensing, SWEcc has refrained
from recommendations or other forms of active involvement. Nevertheless, we have tried
to maintain an awareness of what is going on.
A proposal by the Missouri Society of Professional Engineers is particularly important. A
copy of this proposal was distributed to the SWEcc members present. This proposal
would define a computer engineering examination that would be primarily focused on
software engineering and would, in effect, license software engineers as a specialty
within computer engineering. This proposal will be presented at the national meeting of
the NCEES this Fall as an alternative to the Texas proposal to license software engineers
as a separate discipline.
There was a general perception that the Missouri proposal would be less desirable than
the Texas proposal for software engineering as a profession, possibly serving to divide
the community but also placing software engineering in a less influential and
fundamentally inappropriate position as a subsidiary of computer engineering in the
minds of the professional engineering community. There was discussion of potential
actions that might be taken by SWEcc or the societies in relation to this, such as working
with the professional engineers to develop a joint proposal that would be less detrimental
to software engineering. For reasons discussed above, no action was taken.
4.3 Request for definition of software engineering.
The Texas Board of Professional Engineers asked SWEcc to review their definition of
software engineering and suggest revisions. Individual members and guests.communicated their thoughts on this but no formal response was made, for the reasons
stated above.
5. Code of Ethics Project Report
The Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices Project (SEEPP) asked for
comments on a publicity poster. SWECC agreed that the content was okay, and the
presentation could be improved by consulting with a graphics artist.
Don Gotterbarn gave a report from Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices
Project (SEEPP). The bulk of the report consisted of details about current efforts to promote the
code of ethics by including flyers in ACM Communications and IEEE Software. Due to differences
in publishing techniques, it will not be possible to use the exact same printing process to produce
the flyers for each publication, so the recommendation (accepted by SWEcc) was to amend the
funding plan to have each society pay for the costs of printing and distributing the code in its own
publication.

6. Informal Report on Software Engineering Survey Project
In the absence of a project representative (Laurie Werth is a participant but was not prepared to
represent the project), the SWEcc reviewed information available to us about the status and
progress of this activity. SWEcc members brought up the following issues:
· The report seems to rely solely on results of a survey that has not yet been widely established or
accepted, when there are many existing lists and other sources of information available. For
credibility, it would seem that existing sources ought to be used to populate a baseline version of
the report, with the survey data being used to augment and correct that information.
· There is little information available about whether the survey methodology is working
· It is unclear how the data in the survey are verified
Action Item 4-5 Notify survey project leaders that we wish to meet with them via conference call
to discuss their plans, as well as the issues of using existing sources, methodology, and
verification [Frailey]
7. Informal Report on Education Project
We were informed that Software Engineering Education Project (SWEEP) had had a recent
meeting but there was no formal report available yet. Laurie Werth did report that the following
items were discussed:
· Accreditation criteria for technical and advanced degrees
· A philosophy document
· Presentations at various meetings.· A draft curriculum
· A working plan for the long awaited workshop on software engineering education (apparently
there has been some progress in getting NSF support, but more needs to be done)
SWEcc communicated the following to Laurie:
· SWEcc believes the relationship between the undergraduate curriculum recommendations and
SWEBOK need to be definitized.
· SWEEP should copy its minutes to SEPEP so that the code of ethics issues that relate to
curriculum and such can be coordinated
· SWEcc would like to meet with SWEEP via conference call to review their plans and the issues
mentioned above
8. SWEBOK Project Report
Pierre Bourque and Robert Dupuis reported on the status and plans of the Software Engineering
Body of Knowledge Project (SWEBOK) project. Although there have been some relatively minor
schedule slips due to delayed responses by certain authors, progress is nearly complete on the
current, ironman phase of the project. Highlights include:
· The third (and final) round of the review process is in full swing, with several hundred reviewers
participating. These reviewers come from over 50 countries and represent both the research and
practitioner communities. SWEcc asked SWEBOK to evaluate the demographics of the reviewer
community and report on these to the SWEBOK advisory board at its next meeting.
· Workshops are planned at both CSEET and STC to discuss the BOK with a wider audience and
gain additional feedback from the education and practitioner communities, respectively. [Editor's
note: good attendance was reported at both events, with over 300 at STC.]
· All actions and decisions remain open and accessible to the public through the SWEBOK web
site
· A final document is expected to be completed this summer, with a target effective date of January
1, 2001 after approval by the sponsoring societies.
There was general discussion of the SWEBOK activities, including possible uses of the material
as a resource by teachers and practitioners.
Resolution: SWEcc thanks the SWEBOK team for the quality of its work and its contribution to
software engineering as a profession.
