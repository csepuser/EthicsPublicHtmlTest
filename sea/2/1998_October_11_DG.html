Here is were we stand and what we have been through.

As you know you all were invited to participate in the IEEE formal review
process model, but only some chose to participate.

The balloting group of 146 was formed and they voted on Code version 4.0. There were many comments which were helpful and several negative votes. The executive committee reviewed all responses and modified the Code were it was reasonable to do so. This generated version 5.0 which was recirculated to the balloting group so that they could change their votes if we met their objections or change their votes to negative if we did something new that they did not like. The details of the first vote and the outstanding negative comments can be found at

http://computer.org/standard/sesc/Ethics/

This was the first response.
Resolved Negative Votes: 13/146 (8.9%)
Unresolved Negative Votes: 20/146 (13.7%)
Total Affirmative Votes: 126/146 (86.3%)

I do not have the official final word, but 6 of the unresolved negative votes changed to positive so I think the final analysis is 132/146
affirmative (90%). That is a passing grade :-)

The Steering Committee is presenting the Code with their positive recomendation for approval to the ACM Council this month and to the IEEE
next month.

The current version of the Code is available at the SEERI web site.

It may be a great Thanksgiving.

Don

NEWS SOURCES

Newspaper articles are easy to find and they give a brief synopsis of the RstoryS. They do not often go into enough detail to use heavily in your research papers and projects. Major newspapers usually go into more depth than the local paper. Still as Information Technology (IT) issues become increasing important the papers are devoting more space to these topics. Monday is often Rcomputer dayS in newspaper Business sections. The NY Times has recently added a RCircuitsS special computer news section each Thursday as well. Major newspapers have added web sites where they often go into more detail and/or have additional stories. e.g. use specific newspapers such as the New York Times
www.nytimes.com
or their computer daily news service
http://www.computernewsdaily.com/
Other good candidates include: The Washington Post, The Wall St. Journal,
...

Dear Colleague,

This list is focused on the social and ethical impacts of information and communication technologies. It promotes an inclusive approach to the subject and can be used for discussion, calls for participation and announcements. The list is intended to be of interest to academics, individuals, businesses, communities, organisations and those involved in government at local, national and international levels.

The scope of interest of the list is the full range of actual and potential impacts of computing and related technologies on society and its citizens. This interest can be expressed through items of relevance to
research, to teaching and to consultancy.

While some of the information will be primarily of interest to academics, and the tone is largely academic, participation by non-academics is warmly welcomed.


To join computer-ethics send the following command, (typing your own personal names instead of firstname(s) and lastname)

join computer-ethics firstname(s) lastname

as the only text in the body of an email addressed to:

mailbase@mailbase.ac.uk

Further information about the list can be found on the Centre for Computing and Social Responsibility website at http://www.ccsr.cms.dmu.ac.uk/discussion/ and at the pages linked from it.
