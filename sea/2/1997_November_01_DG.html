Subject: objections to Proposed Code of Ethics

Felipe and Dennis (and Don),

You have asked ACM SIGSOFT for its comments on the draft Code of Ethics. I have asked my Executive Committee for comments, and I hope some will be forthcoming.

In any case, I have some of my own comments, which I include below. And, because some of my concerns are (to me at least) quite serious, let me take this opportunity to state clearly that until some of these problems are clarified and/or fixed, that ACM SIGSOFT does *not* endorse this draft code.

In general, I think that my comments fall into two categories:

*General comments about ethics (such as financial conflict), where I may be especially naive.

*Comments about places where I think the code is too naive about some special properties of software and the software industry.

I'm less worried about the first category than the second because it would, if I am correct, significantly reduce the collection of people that might pay attention to and learn from the proposed code.

Feel free to pass these comments around as you wish (preferably including this message as context). I suspect that they sound far more negative than they should, but perhaps they will generate some useful improvements.

David ***** I've just read the draft of the software engineering code of ethics. And, indeed, I have a couple of comments.

1) There is not a single mention of the issue of modifying or changing existing software. Since this is a significant piece of the industry, the omission is obvious and serious. I don't believe that adding in a few words can handle this easily, either, because in many cases the pressures to make changes without a "full" understanding of the software are real and are reasonable.

Another take on this is that the ability to change and evolve software is one of the primary characteristics that distinguishes software from other engineering disciplines. If we don't account for the distinguishing characteristics of software, then we can use "any old engineering ethics code."

2) My reading is that the code implicitly focuses on contract software, where specifications and design documents play a critical and direct role. However, a significant amount of the industry now works in the shrinkwrap world, where these issues are wildly different. Yes, specification exists. Yes, design exists. But phrases like "satisfy the user's requirements", "have the client's approval", "understand fully the specifications for software on which they work", etc., are at best difficult to interpret and are at worst meaningless.

3) 1.06 talks about "effective procedures for promotion of quality and reduction of risk." Seems reasonable, for sure. But what about a situation where a company (quite reasonably) chooses a niche in which their immediate goal is to produce a software product that is likely to be low quality but first on the market? Or what about a company that intentionally takes a high?risk strategy (perhaps, for example, depending on other products that aren't yet completed)? Perhaps situations like these are covered indirectly in the draft code, but I don't believe that this is sufficient.

4) 1.14 talks about making tradeoffs visible to all parties concerned, including the public. And 6.13 talks about sharing "useful software?related knowledge, inventions, or discoveries with the profession". If the draft code doesn't make it clear that companies and software engineers have interests???in particular, certain classes of intellectual property???that needn't be disclosed, or that must be disclosed with care, then it will not be of use to many software engineering practitioners. You address this somewhat in Principle 4, especially 4.05, but explicitly observing this inherent set of conflicts seems of value.

5) 1.15 talks about departing from appropriate industry standards "only when technically justified." How about economically justifications? Even more critical, are there reasonable and sufficient industry standards that apply to most or all areas of software engineering? Safety?critical software? Real?time embedded software? Shrinkwrap software? Operating system software? Transaction processing software? Game and entertainment software? Internet?based software? Etc. If not, is it "ethical" to provide a principle that is unsatisfiable?

6) 3.07 discusses avoiding financial conflicts of interest. Well, I'm no expert here, but let me give you two examples where such conflict would exist according to 3.07, but where it seems ludicrous.

a) I was once on a program committee for a software engineering conference. I was advocating the acceptance of a videotape by a person at a company for a presentation. I was asked by another committee member if I had stock in the company. Well, it turns out I did (I'd guess it was worth under $1500 at the time), although it hadn't occurred to me. Was I unethical? Nope. Was there an appearance of being unethical? Well, perhaps. Would the conference (and the profession) have been better had I recused myself? Surely not. Indeed, given that almost everyone in the community owns mutual funds that own high?tech stocks, I'll wager that this kind of broad "financial interest" conflict would make it all but impossible to make progress in many bodies.

(Professional standards committees would, I suspect, fall apart entirely. Why? Because all industrial members would, according to 3.07, have an ethical conflict if their employers would benefit from their involvement???and this is, of course, in some sense the primary goal of sending employees to serve on standardization boards.)

b) The State of Washington has a new and extremely firm ethics code. Hunter Simpson was a member of the state's Information System's Board (ISB), a gubenatorial appointee. He's been CEO of a company or two and is quite wealthy. He owned $9K of IBM stock. He was involved in a (unanimous) decision to award a state contract to IBM. He was ultimately punished by the State Ethics Board for having a conflict of interest. This was (although you might not be able to tell from my brief description) crazy as can be. If applied even more aggressively (easy to believe in our state's case) you could imagine situations in which the only people who could sit on the state's ISB are people who have no assets and have no knowledge of technology. OK, I'm sure that these are not what you had in mind. And I suspect (again) that these kind of "exceptions" are covered elsewhere in the draft. (And I'm sure you have way better examples than mine, since I am confident you've thought about this.) But 3.07 is simply too simplistic, in my opinion.

7) 6.07 says to "only accept remuneration appropriate to professional qualifications or experience." Forbes (I think) just printed its list of the top 100 richest people in technology. Many of them got rich essentially as software engineers who started companies. I'd hate to be in the middle of a discussion about whether they are accepted appropriate remuneration.
