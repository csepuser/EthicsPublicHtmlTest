Interview of Tony Cocchi


Interviewer: Michael Davis (MD)
Interviewee: Tony (Anthony) Cocchi (TC)
Date Approved: Nov 12, 2002 (edited but not approved)
Location : IBM Watson Research Center, Yorktown Heights, NY

1. What is your educational background?

TC: B. EE. (Pratt Institute), 1964: M. EE (Pratt Institute), 1967; and M. CS. (New York Polytechnic), 1987.

2. What sort of organization do you work for? What do you do there?

TC: I work at IBM in a research center. My research concerns Java virtual machine systems.

3. What experience, if any, have you had in software development? If you are a software developer, what led you into that field?

TC: For 35 years, I have worked mostly on development of software (doing some management and teaching along the way). The software was for use in a research environment. I did everything from start to finish, from conception to application. Developing software is a subset of what I do. I started out working with hardware (as an EE), but I found software much more interesting. I didn’t like having to worry about physical stuff like the speed of light.

4. Are you an engineer?

TC: Yes—unless you use my distinction between engineer and scientist. An engineer delivers things on time, on budget, and on function. Scientists do experimental work. I do experimental work.

5. How did you hear about the IEEE/ACM Joint Task force on Software Engineering and Professional Practice (SEEPP)?

TC: I heard about SEEPP from Pat Douglas. She asked me to help with the technical side of something she was working on, maybe in 1992. She’s an educational psychologist (though she works for IBM). Pat and I met with Mario Barbacci. The focus of our discussion was the Body of Knowledge. Pat had a plan for structuring the body of knowledge in a hierarchy: new hire, experienced, expert, manager, and so on. Though the Body of Knowledge was only one of the three parts of SEEPP’s effort (the other two being education and ethics, I think), I don’t recall any mention of ethics at that meeting. I had the impression that SEEPP had been started, but that not much was going on.

6. What led you to participate in SEEPP’s work?

TC: I didn’t. I was involved with documenting the Body of Knowledge. Pat and I co-chaired a sub-group for the Body of Knowledge. I had no part in SEEPP’s steering committee or in any activity having to do with the code of ethics.

7. Were you familiar with codes of ethics before you became involved in SEEPP? Explain.

TC: No.

8. In what ways did you participate in SEEPP’s work, especially in the process of preparing the code? (The more details, the better.)

TC: None. See 5 above.

9. By what means did you participate? For example, did you participate by email, or by phone, or through face-to-face meetings, or by letter, or by informal conversation, or the like?

TC: We did interviews for the body of knowledge: face-to-face. Email didn’t work well for that. We had so much to explain before we could get useful answers.

10. Did any of these means of participation seem to work better than the others? Any seem to work worse? Which would you recommend as best? Why?

TC: My experience is that face-to-face is much better than email, phone, or anything else. No question about it.

11. Any events that particularly stick in your mind relevant to the process? (The more details, the better.)

TC: Not events, but two insights. First, the body of knowledge changes constantly. We could not just state it and be done. We had to build a process that would iterate indefinitely to keep the body of knowledge up to date. Second, the job of constructing the body of knowledge (and of keeping it up to date) was too big to be done entirely by volunteers working on it in their spare time. There is a need for an organization with employees devoted to it full time.

12. Do you have any documents, paper or electronic, relevant to your participation in the process? May we have a copy?

TC: No.

13. Has your thinking about codes of ethics changed as a result of your participation in SEEPP’s work? How?

TC: Nothing relevant to code of ethics

14. What, in your opinion, is important about having a code of ethics?

TC: No opinion.

15. Is there anything about your participation that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

TC: No.

16. Is there anything about the final code that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

TC: I have never looked at the code.

17. Is there anyone whose participation in the process seems to you especially important? Explain.

TC: For body of knowledge: Pat Douglas had the discipline and model to give the project a form we could work with.

18. Anyone who you think we should be sure to talk to? Explain.

TC: No idea.

19. If you had been in charge of the process, what, if anything, would you have done differently?

TC: No idea.

20. Is there anything we should have asked but didn’t? Anything you want to add to what you have already said?

TC: No.
