Interview of Ben Fairweather


Interviewer: Michael Davis
Interviewee: Ben Fairweather (BF)
Date Approved: February 25, 2003
Location : De Montfort University, Leicester, England

1. What is your educational background?

BF: Ph.D. in Moral Philosophy, University of Wales, College of Cardiff (1996). My dissertation was on the relation of Green Moral and Political Theory to Liberal Moral and Political Theory. I also have an MA from the University of York (1990) in political theory. My master’s thesis was on the idea of toleration. My BA in Politics (1989) was from the University of New Castle-Upon-Tyne. I started out as BSc [bachelor of science] in computing, but switched after one year.

2. What sort of organization do you work for? What do you do there?

BF: I work at the Centre for Computing and Social Responsibility (CCSR). It’s part of the School of Computing here. The centre does research and education in the ethical and social issues related to information and communication technologies. I do applied philosophy. The Centre hired me because they thought explaining what “social responsibility” meant was in part a philosophical problem. I also teach, within the School of Computing. [What do you teach?]

3. What experience, if any, have you had in software development? If you are a software developer, what led you into that field?

BF:The only experience I have with software development was doing student projects as an undergraduate.

4. Are you an engineer?

BF:No. I was not trained as an engineer, nor am I qualified by experience—although, over the years, I’ve gained some experience that would be relevant.

5. How did you hear about the IEEE/ACM Joint Task force on Software Engineering and Professional Practice (SEEPP)?

BF:When Don [Gotterbarn] visited here, January 1997. Don told me what he was doing. He gave me a draft of the code and asked for comment. I gave feedback.

6. What led you to participate in SEEPP’s work?

BF:I like to be cooperative. I was then relatively new in post. So, it was something to get my teeth in. I had an interest in codes of ethics from my Ph.D. I had a chapter on the role of business, including its limited liability. [Is this right?]

7. Were you familiar with codes of ethics before you became involved in SEEPP? Explain.

BF: I’ve certainly become much more familiar since then. But I was already familiar with some codes in 1997, some codes of business ethics, but not codes of professional ethics. I was then aware that professional codes existed but I had not looked at any.

8. In what ways did you participate in SEEPP’s work, especially in the process of preparing the code? (The more details, the better.)

BF:I think I got my ordering of events wrong [in 5 above]. Within a few weeks after I took up my post here, there was a conference, ETHICOMP ’96—one of a series of annual conferences dealing with ethics in computing. (The first one, held the year before, was at De Montfort. Since then, there was one in the Netherlands, one in Poland, and so on. Each conference is held in a different place.) The ‘96 one was in Madrid. Prof. Barroso gave a paper, “The PAPA of Computer Ethics”, in Spanish. “PAPA” stands for “Privacy, Accuracy, Property, and Accessibility.” When Don arrived in January 1997, I was working on a response to Barroso’s paper (“Why an Incomplete Code is Worse than None at All”). I had been looking at the codes linked to CCSP’s website. So, I was ready to look at another code—and I had some strong ideas about what a code should look like.

9. By what means did you participate? For example, did you participate by email, or by phone, or through face-to-face meetings, or by letter, or by informal conversation, or the like?

BF: I generally worked with paper drafts of the code. There were frequent face-to-face meetings with Don and Simon [Rogerson]. There were also emails between Don and me. I also received emails sent to the SEEPP listserve.

10. Did any of these means of participation seem to work better than the others? Any seem to work worse? Which would you recommend as best? Why?

BF: I think the face-to-face meetings were useful, but only in conjunction with drafts passed to me on paper or electronically.

11. Any events that particularly stick in your mind relevant to the process? (The more details, the better.)

BF: Two. One occasion that sticks in my mind is calling on Don in an office here, talking about phrases I’d proposed and Don wasn’t happy with. [DEEP ARCHIVE THE FOLLOWING:] The other occasion I’m a bit wary of talking about here: I tried to have my name withdrawn from the list of signers. I felt the short version undermined the completeness of the code. It could be used without the long version, thus undermining the completeness of the code I thought necessary to avoid misinterpretation and abuse. Don agreed to take my name off, but it ended up there anyway.

12. Do you have any documents, paper or electronic, relevant to your participation in the process? May we have a copy?

BF: Yes. I will try to find and send. Please send me reminders.

13. Has your thinking about codes of ethics changed as a result of your participation in SEEPP’s work? How?

BF: Yes. I think I became much more aware of the value of the short version. What we need is a hypertext version. It would begin with the short code with visual cues to look further. Touch a key word and you would be taken to the relevant provisions in the long version. Touch one of the provisions in the long version and you would be taken to some cases or articles interpreting that provision. A code is better than no attention to ethics. But there are other approaches that would be better than a code, for example, incorporating ethics into courses.

14. What, in your opinion, is important about having a code of ethics?

BF: What is important is for there to be an honest attempt to uphold the code, understand it, and see it as a way into morality—rather than as a statement of morality.

15. Is there anything about your participation that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

BF:I’m unhappy that I failed to prevent the short version from having the free-standing role it eventually got. What I was pleased with was that I always felt my opinions were being taken seriously. There was always a reasonable chance that what I suggested would be taken on board—if it seemed an improvement.

16. Is there anything about the final code that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

BF: I’m pleased with how well the full code deals with incompleteness. The Preamble provides the right sort of guide to how to use the code. What I’m not pleased about is the short version. I also didn’t much like the publicity materials. There are no visual cues to let someone who sees the code know of the depth behind it, both reference works and education. We need to think about ways to help people see the code in context.

17. Is there anyone whose participation in the process seems to you especially important? Explain.

BF:Yes, Don Gotterbarn.

18. Anyone who you think we should be sure to talk to? Explain.

BF: Gotterbarn. No other opinion. I’m not aware of the involvement of anyone else—except for the people here, of course.

19. If you had been in charge of the process, what, if anything, would you have done differently?

BF:I don’t think I’d have pursued a code at all. I ‘d have pursued education or ethical questions.

20. Is there anything we should have asked but didn’t? Anything you want to add to what you have already said?

BF:No.
