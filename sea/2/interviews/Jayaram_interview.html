Interview with Nayarana Jayaram


Interviewer: Michael Davis
Interviewee: Nayarana Jayaram
Date Approved: February 25, 2003 (approved)
Location : Berjaya Eden Park Hotel, 35-39 Inverness Terrace, London, England

1. What is your educational background?

BS in EE, MS in EE and PhD in CS
Experience in academia and industry for over 35 years.
Held two patents in microprocessor hardware.

2. What sort of organization do you work for? What do you do there?

NJ: I now teach computer science at London Metropolitan University. I also am active in research and consulting.

3. What experience, if any, have you had in software development? If you are a software developer, what led you into that field?

NJ: As electronic engineering went from circuit boards to software, I did the same. By 1975, I was concentrating on software. [See 1 above.] From 1977-80, I did research, in Mysore, on parallel processing. I also was a consultant to small and medium enterprises, especially concerning software for control systems. By 1980, I was Senior Software Engineer at KTM Electronics. 1982-86, I worked on software for condition monitoring for BP [British Petroleum].

4. Are you an engineer?

NJ: I don’t know. I’m not particular about title. The title “software engineer” is okay with me. That’s where my contributions are. What is the difference between information systems and software engineering? In the UK, we seem to call people who work on information systems “systems analysts” rather than “software engineers”.

5. How did you hear about the IEEE/ACM Joint Task force on Software Engineering and Professional Practice (SEEPP)?

NJ: I was then chair of the IEEE-CS UK-Ireland Chapter—and a member of ACM since 1987. Mario Baracci emailed me about SEEPP. We were acquainted, but I think he emailed me about it because he wanted to contact the chapter. That would have been sometime in 1993, or early 1994, I think.

6. What led you to participate in SEEPP’s work?

NJ: Good question. Two things. First is my own personal life. My son had a rare medical disease. In the course of dealing with his doctors, I had become aware of medical ethics. Second, I had long been troubled by the unrealistic timetables and resources set for software projects on which I worked and was brought in to consult about. The problem seemed more than technical. The invitation from SEEPP was an invitation to think in a systematic way about issues already on my mind, how something like medical ethics might be developed for software engineering.

7. Were you familiar with codes of ethics before you became involved in SEEPP? Explain.

NJ: No. Well, to some extent I was. I had to be familiar with the medical code [see 6 above]. Also, I am a high-caste Brahmin, the son of a professor who was very ethical. For Brahmins, the public good is what one should aim at. We are teachers. Our job is to spread knowledge, not to get rich. We have our own code.

8. In what ways did you participate in SEEPP’s work, especially in the process of preparing the code? (The more details, the better.)

NJ: I collected British codes and did a comparison. That would have been at the end of 1993 or the beginning of 1994. We wanted to see what the state of the art was.

9. By what means did you participate? For example, did you participate by email, or by phone, or through face-to-face meetings, or by letter, or by informal conversation, or the like?

NJ: Overwhelmingly by email.

10. Did any of these means of participation seem to work better than the others? Any seem to work worse? Which would you recommend as best? Why?

NJ: Email is for preparation (setting the scene), but you need face-to-face meetings to develop the final project. The fundamentals should be done by email, but the finer points require face-to-face meetings. I’d have preferred two face-to-face meetings, one in Europe and one in US.

11. Any events that particularly stick in your mind relevant to the process? (The more details, the better.)

NJ: I will have to look at my diary first. I kept those from the period. Right now all that sticks in my memory is a debate over the term “economic disadvantage”.

12. Do you have any documents, paper or electronic, relevant to your participation in the process? May we have a copy?

NJ: I may have something on paper. I will look. I’m sure I don’t have anything on my disk drives. Every time I change institutions, I have to wipe my disk drives clean. It’s to protect intellectual property.

13. Has your thinking about codes of ethics changed as a result of your participation in SEEPP’s work? How?

NJ: Certainly. I think of codes as life process. I agree that getting the language right is important. But I now think it’s more important what you do with the code once you have it. What influence can it have? Software engineering has also changed a lot in just the few years since the code was adopted. I now think we didn’t emphasize maintenance enough.

14. What, in your opinion, is important about having a code of ethics?

NJ: It’s having some kind of soul for the profession. A profession is not alive without ethics. The code is part of giving a profession a soul.

15. Is there anything about your participation that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

NJ: I was pleased to be able to interact with people working on such a project and to be able to make some contribution. Participating was a very rich experience.


16. Is there anything about the final code that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

NJ: I don’t think I’m unhappy with anything. We were busy, too busy to do more. What was lacking was more encouragement from the organization. We should have had a chance to meet the people we were working with. The societies should have found the money for one or two face-to-face meetings.

17. Is there anyone whose participation in the process seems to you especially important? Explain.

NJ: Ed Mechler. He saved the project when it seemed to have fallen apart. He kept us going until we drafted the code (version 1). Everything after that was minor in comparison.

18. Anyone who you think we should be sure to talk to? Explain.

NJ: Ed Mechler

19. If you had been in charge of the process, what, if anything, would you have done differently?

NJ: I would have had one or two face-to-face meetings, with budget from IEEE/ACM: One meeting relatively early in the process and conference or workshop when the code was wrapping up. The conference or workshop would have been open to all, with participants in drafting the code making some of the presentations. The purpose would have been to help disseminate the code.

20. Is there anything we should have asked but didn’t? Anything you want to add to what you have already said?

NJ: No.
