Interview of Robert Melford


Interviewer: Michael Davis (MD) and Tony Spencer (TS)
Interviewee: Robert Melford (RM)
Date Approved: October 31, 2002 (edited but not approved)
Location : Hof’s Hun restaurant, Irvine, CA

1. What is your educational background?

I have about 2 years of study at Cal State Northridge. I left school to earn a living. I was studying economics. I have some formal education in economics and some in programming. I am fully self-educated in systems management as well as the business side of computing operations. I am fully self-educated in networking, the technical aspects of networking, as well as the ethics. One of my incentives for leaving school was that I wasn’t able to get what I was after. At the time, there was basically nothing available on computing security. I originally went in to study electrical engineering, but found that that wasn’t quite my forte. I came into computing in a backward sort of way. I read some issues of Scientific American in the late ‘70s or early ‘80s. I believe it was the September 1980 issue that was devoted to automation in the workplace, and the following issue in October was dedicated to software computing. Those two articles were extremely influential on me. They made me think about automating the business workplace. During that time, I had taken several courses in programming and management of information systems (MIS). I found that I had a knack for it. I also took programming classes that were oriented toward business majors, towards accounting students. That was at Cal State Northridge. When I left school I decided that I wanted to continue my education on my own. So I went and bought a PDD11. Yes, that was the computer Digital Equipment Corporation then made, the old deck PDD11, forerunner of the Vax. I mounted an early version of UNIX on it, and started to go from there. That led into some consultant work. I started my own consulting practice in 1984. I did consulting for 15-16 years. About 1999, I started with my current employer as chief information officer. I focused on operational aspects of computing. That led into concerns about reliability and quality of service, which led me into areas of security. I spent a lot of time in security. Because of my business background, I realized that this wasn’t just a technical issue; it was also an issue of people, management, and behavior. In the late 80s, I was encouraged by a colleague to join IEEE and also IEEE’s computer society (CS-IEEE). At some point, I got a mailing from the CS-IEEE saying that they were starting up an ethics subcommittee and asking if I was interested in participating. I said yes, and I was contacted by Ralph Preiss. At that time he was the chairman of the Committee on Public Policy (COPP). That was around the time of the Robert Morris, Jr., Internet worm. Well, the computer society was asked to make a statement on this. I think Ralph Preiss was the chair at the time. We wrote a position piece on ethics. We also incorporated into our piece issues about viruses. I think this was around 1989. The board of governors of CS-IEEE rejected the statement we wrote in its entirety. And that was our first understanding that those of us in the computing profession and engineering profession by and large have a discomfort with dealing with soft issues, like ethics. Trying to suggest that there is a common right way of behaving can be controversial in a technical profession. They were very uncomfortable about making such a public statement. I would later learn that this discomfort was also very much a creature of the bureaucratic, or institutional, behavior within the computer society and IEEE. In other words, if a certain issue isn’t taken on by the board of governors, it will never progress forward. That was also a learning experience.

2. What sort of organization do you work for? What do you do there?

I work for St. George Integrate Financial Solutions (IFS). It’s a financial services company that was founded in 1999. It provides smaller companies with the type of technical and professional resources and experiences that are generally only available to very large institutions. The company’s mission is to allow smaller financial institutions to compete on an even playing field against the Bank of America, Citicorp, and American Express. We provide the kind of technologies—like credit scoring and credit analysis—that are available in a highly automated environment at large institutions but that smaller institutions must still do with pencil and paper or at best a spread sheet, with a person or a group of people making a judgmental decision. We also provide credit checks, consulting and marketing services, and things like that. I am the chief information officer. I am responsible for the day-to-day operations of our corporate computing environment. We have five offices that are linked together by virtual private networks.

3. What experience, if any, have you had in software development? If you are a software developer, what led you into that field?

Yes, I have a lot of experience with software development. During the course of my years as a consultant, which ran from 84-99, the main goal of the consulting practice was systems integration. I partnered with a colleague in 1997 to develop a program for data mining for banks. The program was designed to help banks determine the amount of profit that customers generate for the bank. The program is called Wallet Share. This program is actually how I got my current job at St. George; the company had taken notice of what we had done.

4. Are you an engineer?

Not officially. I’m not licensed and I don’t have a degree. But most people would consider me to be an engineer. I think most of my colleagues view me as such. I certainly identify with that side of my field, probably more than anything else other than general business. I am an engineer in the sense that I determine how technology can be applied in a cohesive and complimentary manner to solve problems.

5. How did you hear about the IEEE/ACM Joint Task force on Software Engineering and Professional Practice (SEEPP)?

There is a long and a short answer to your question. The long answer involves telling you how the committee came into being in the first place. The short answer is that I was the chairman at the time of the committee on public policy for the computer society. And, because I had some significant positions (related to ethics and computing) in the computer society, I was asked if I would assist in starting up the ethics side. I heard about the project because I was already involved in the computer society.

6. What lead you to participate in SEEPP’s work?

I was interested in the subject of ethics. Because I came to computing from a business background, I understood that it’s how people apply technology (how they use it) that matters. I was brought into the team for two reasons: First, I was chairing the committee on public policy at the time. I was the natural bureaucratic person to assign to it. Second, I had written and spoken on ethics, and my CV was known to the computer society’s board of governors. I agreed to co-chair SEEPP because I was interested in it and I was the obvious choice because of my experience. I don’t think anyone gets involved in ethics unless they are interested in it. I know that’s why I got involved.

7. Were you familiar with codes of ethics before you became involved in SEEPP? Explain.

I was modestly familiar with codes of ethics. I had spent a fair amount of time researching and learning about the IEEE code—and the ACM code. I had looked at codes of medical ethics. I’ve studied the bar association’s code of ethics. The latter was part of my general research and learning in computer security. I wrote several papers, and had to do some background research in understanding that.

8. In what ways did you participate in SEEPP’s work, especially in the process of preparing the code? (The more details, the better.)

Well, I didn’t participate in preparing the code itself. Because Don Gotterbarn and I were the two founding chairs of SEEPP, my main participation was in setting up this agenda, recruiting people to the committee, and laying out our work agendas. I spent a lot of time doing the back room politics. I spent a lot of time with my colleagues from the IEEE leadership assuring them that we were not going to reinvent the IEEE code of ethics. I spent a lot of time within the computer society assuring the computer society board of governors that we were not going to make public statements that might embarrass the computer society. I spent a lot of time basically putting out fires. I played the classic middle manager role by keeping all of the nonsense away from people who were trying to get the work done. I had to discontinue my involvement in late 96, early 97, because my own work had gotten too crazy.

9. By what means did you participate? For example, did you participate by email, or by phone, or through face-to-face meetings, or by letter, or by informal conversation, or the like?

As with any team, we met face-to-face, on the phone, and by e-mail. The team consisted of Pat Sullivan, Michael McFarland, and Ramon Barquin (one of the founders of the Computer Ethics Institute). We met in a number of locations. We met at computer society headquarters at least once (Washington, DC), if not several times. We met at BU [Boston University] at least once during my tenure. And at that point McFarland had us go back to the committee. I know I met with Professor Susie Wiseman [Weisband?] at the University of Arizona. I don’t remember if we had the whole team out at U of A. We might have, but I don’t recall.

10. Did any of these means of participation seem to work better than the others? Any seem to work worse? Which would you recommend as best? Why?

Face-to-face meetings were critical. At least during my tenure, we hardly ever got work done otherwise. When we met face-to-face, we got a lot of stuff done.

11. Any events that particularly stick in your mind relevant to the process? (The more details, the better.)

The first meeting at the computer society headquarters. That was very productive meeting. Don, Susie, McFarland, and Fletcher Buckley were there. And our attorney was there. He was based out of New Jersey. Elliott Chikofsky was there. He didn’t participate in SEEPP, but he was one of the speakers. Steve Barber was the name of the lawyer.

12. Do you have any documents, paper or electronic, relevant to your participation in the process? May we have a copy?

Yes, lots. I will check. What I want to do is go back and look through what I have here [in the file he was holding], make sure it’s not something that I really want to have confidential, and then all I would want to know is if I release it to you, I’d be able to have access to it.

13. Has your thinking about codes of ethics changed as a result of your participation in SEEPP’s work? How?

They’re easier to write than practice statements. A code of ethics also makes it simpler when you’re starting to write practice statements.

14. What, in your opinion, is important about having a code of ethics?

That’s a chicken-or-egg question; so, I’ll give you a chicken-or-egg answer. A code doesn’t have any importance if people don’t understand it, think about it, and apply it. The main importance is to clarify thinking and raise awareness. Awareness of a problem is always required before you can solve it.

15. Is there anything about your participation that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

I’m thrilled that I had a chance to participate in something that I think was pretty important. I’m displeased that just when things were starting to get interesting, I had to pull out. And I had to pull out just because my regular work didn’t allow me the time to continue. That was disappointing. I felt like I let the team down.


16. Is there anything about the final code that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

I was happy, and am happy, with the code. I think it’s a well-thought-out document. I didn’t find any gaps that should have been addressed. There’s no item or aspect of the code that I think could have been better. So I can’t give an affirmation about that.

17. Is there anyone whose participation in the process seems to you especially important? Explain.

Obviously Don. Michael McFarland and Ramon Barquin were also very influential on me. Pat Sullivan was also very helpful. But Don has done the yeoman’s work. Without Don, nothing would have been done. Without Fletcher Buckley, the person who originally kicked things off, it would have taken a lot longer for any of this to get going. Fletcher was great, though.

18. Anyone who you think we should be sure to talk to? Explain.

Add to the names in 17, the following: Ron Anderson, Vanderbilt University; Albert Howard; and Fiorenza….(*It’s probably best for Melford to provide the other names to you with their correct spelling—TS.)

19. If you had been in charge of the process, what, if anything, would you have done differently?

Since I was in charge of the process, let me change the question. If I had been in charge of the process that was in charge of SEEPP itself, what would I have done differently? If I had access to greater funds, I would have made it much easier to bring the teams face-to-face to work on things. If we could have had more face-to-face meetings that were closer together, less time in between, we would have progressed much further, much farther, much more quickly. I would also have put more effort into addressing all of the folks who did not want to deal with the soft issues and who were concerned that the committee would embarrass them.

20. Is there anything we should have asked but didn’t? Anything you want to add to what you have already said?

It would be interesting to discuss personal dynamics that went on in the deliberations. Certainly, much of what we accomplished was influenced by the kinds of personalities involved. Our motivations, our working together, so on.
