Code of Ethics feedback Email from G.M. Harding to Gotterbarn: "Code of Ethics feedback" - November 18, 1997 (Level 2) E-mail

-----BEGIN PGP SIGNED MESSAGE----

I am glad that the Code of Ethics is being revisited, and its scope expanded beyond the ACM. This is, I know, difficult and often thankless work, but it's necessary if organizations like ACM and IEEE/CS are to become true governing bodies of the profession. The alternative ?? governmental certification and licensing ?? is far worse, and I'm convinced that the time is fast approaching when we will have to demonstrate the ability to govern ourselves or be subjected to the heavy hand of Big Brother.

That said, I have deep-seated reservations about several provisions of the current Draft, and I hope that the Executive Committee contemplates a complete rework after comments are re? ceived. In its current state, this Draft is nowhere near ready for release.

Perhaps my most fundamental reservation has to do with the fact that no enforcement mechanisms are prescribed. As the Pre? amble suggests, ethics is not an exact science; it's a subjec? tive matter, and no enumeration of "principles" can transform it into an objective discipline. Codes of ethics are not state? ments on the nature of abstract "right" and "wrong"; rather, they are statements of feelings shared by certain individuals at certain points in time. This Draft attempts to describe how members of ACM and IEEE/CS feel about professional standards in the year 1997. The reason for making it a public document is to give laypersons some idea of what those feelings are, and thus of what they can expect when dealing with a professional in the field of software engineering.

If a code of ethics is to have any meaning at all, it must offer remedies to people who believe it has been violated, and the only appropriate way to claim such remedies is to appeal to the authority which promulgated the code. In this case, such an appeal would be made to ACM, IEEE/CS, or another professional society subscribing to the code. The disciplinary tools at the disposal of such societies range from warnings and reprimands to suspension or expulsion. Organizations use these tools, and subscribe to codes of ethics, because they seek to assure the public that their members will act in certain ways, and that those who do not will lose their standing as members. Such as? surances are the alternative to governmental regulation.

At the very minimum, therefore, the code must require of subscribing organizations that they maintain a compliance body charged with receiving and evaluating complaints against their members. To ensure fairness, basic procedural safeguards should also be outlined.

For the sake of discussion, let me offer the proposal of inserting a new Principle, "Governing Bodies," between existing Principles 7 and 8. The inclusion of such a Principle would make it clear that we, as software professionals, consider ourselves uniquely qualified to govern our own, and that we are asserting our intention to do so. The new Principle would deal with the relationship of subscribing organizations to the code, and of members to their governing organizations. It would set forth the basic obligations on both sides, including the obligation of professional organizations to evaluate complaints against their members and the right of members to be judged by impar? tial peers.

I realize that addressing these matters will necessitate a substantial amount of rework, and I deeply regret it. However, by the terms of its own charter, the Committee really cannot avoid dealing with the issue of enforcement. The original joint steering committee was charged with establishing "appropriate set(s) of standards for professional practice of software engineering upon which industrial decisions, professional certi? fication, and educational curricula can be based." Certifica? tion presupposes some means of enforcement. Speaking to you as colleagues and fellow professionals, I assure you that failure to make provision for enforcement will result in a code which is essentially irrelevant and universally perceived as such. Consequently, it will do nothing to avert, and may even invite, external regulation of a sort none of us wants.

It will not do to say that enforcement provisions can be left to the subscribing organizations. If our goal is to formu? late standards of professional conduct applicable to the entire range of software practice -- and that should be our goal -- we must insist on a uniform interpretation of those standards. If we do not, the code will be interpreted by the various organizations in ways which diverge over time, eventually resulting in a muddle of conflicting precedents. For this reason, my own preference would be to remove complaint proceedings from the jurisdiction of the subscribing organizations by requiring a multi-organizational composition for all panels of inquiry. Of course, the code of ethics itself need not delve into such procedural details, but it must set forth the general guidelines for its own enforcement.

With regard to complaints against professionals, I'm sure that many respondents will join me in expressing concern about Clause 7.07. This clause appears intended to offer protection against discrimination on the basis of factors unrelated to competence, but if that's the case, the Committee should explicitly say so. (In fact, I find no language condemning such discrimination anywhere in the Draft, and I consider that to be a serious omission.) The specific problem with 7.07 is that it could foster the kind of tacit "code of silence" sometimes associated with physicians and police officers ?? a code which discourages members of the profession from testifying against each other. I'm sure you'll agree that we do not want to dis? courage the prompt identification of incompetent practitioners, or practitioners operating outside their field of competence. Nobody is in a better position to do this than colleagues ?? indeed, they do so now, as a matter of common (if informal) practice. At the very least, therefore, 7.07 should be amended to read something like this:

Not interfere in the professional career progression of any colleague, except in cases where concern for client or public welfare causes a professional to question the competence of a colleague for the work being performed.

It might also be desirable to insert language encouraging the resolution of incompetence cases between colleagues, or be? tween professionals and their clients, making it clear that ap? peal to the governing body is to be regarded as a last resort.

Because a code of ethics is about expectations rather than abstract notions of good and bad, I would rewrite the Preamble to eliminate vague references to "good." This is not, after all, a doctrinal creed for a religious body, but a document reflecting the current state of informed thought about optimal practices in the field of software engineering. We should make it clear to the public -- and no less to our own colleagues-- that compliance with the code is not a matter of morality, but a matter of professionalism carrying explicit assurances to the public and explicit consequences for violators.

I find Clause 6.01 troublesome. Does this mean that a competent practitioner who happens to be a Stalinist or a member of the Ku Klux Klan forfeits professional standing? Perhaps we really do want to make that determination, but if so it must be very explicit, and the proscribed affiliations must be clearly spelled out. As a practical matter, it makes more sense to condemn actions than to condemn affiliations, e.g., a theoretical Stalinist may practice, whereas a Pol Pot would be considered worthy of expulsion.

A more mundane reading of 6.01 -- and one the Committee probably intended -- would restrict its applicability to deal? ings in the course of doing business, so that, for example, a professional would decline to deal with a firm known to produce defective software. But this distinction must be made explicit. As it stands, 6.01 is too vague to offer any useful guidance.

Descending to the level of the technical, I'm extremely uncomfortable with Clause 1.12. Perhaps elaboration would make its intent clearer, but as it stands it encourages dangerous practices. To begin with, of course, it's seldom unequivocally clear to a practitioner that a particular element of data is "outdated," or even "flawed." Only the owner of the data can make that determination, and then only with competent technical advice from a professional. Even if the owner of the data deems it outdated or flawed, it's seldom good practice to delete data altogether, because such decisions are irrevocable and not in? frequently regretted. The more prudent course is to "comment out" obsolete data if possible, or move it offline to backup media.

I think perhaps the Committee meant to address a very real concern about privacy. The proliferation of databases contain? ing extensive personal information concerns all of us, and the code should explicitly encourage extreme caution in the collec? tion and use of such data, even, perhaps, to the extent of pro? hibiting the participation of professionals in the collection of personal data which is not properly safeguarded. But that is a different issue. The code must simultaneously foster two things: Utmost respect for the privacy of those about whom per? sonal data is collected, and a high degree of respect for the rights of the owners of data. Each of these is sufficiently im? portant to warrant a Clause of its own.

Finally, a few nits. (There are more, but I don't want to exhaust your patience.)

The wording in 1.11 is awkward. I suggest:

Take care to use only accurate data derived by lawful means, and to use it only in ways authorized by the owner of the data.

(It's a fine point, but "lawful means" is preferable to "legal sources" in this context.)

Passive voice is awkward in 4.09. I suggest:

Represent no interest adverse to their employer's without the employer's specific consent, unless a higher ethical concern is being compromised; and in that case, inform the employer or another appro? priate authority of the engineer's ethical concern.

The authors of the Draft have attempted to avoid the generic masculine by resorting to the plural. Now, I dislike the generic masculine as much as anyone, not so much because it's politically incorrect as because it's inaccurate. However, I've found that the best substitute is the second person singular, which has the advantages of being simple, accurate, and direct. "Software engineers shall ensure that they are qualified..." sounds sterile and remote, as if it applies to someone else. "As a software engineer, you shall ensure..." commands much more attention. I recommend recasting the entire document in the second person.

Thank you for hearing me out. I will follow up this electronic correspondence with hard copy.

G. M. HARDING ACM 2200236
