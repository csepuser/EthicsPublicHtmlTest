On Wed, 9 Apr 1997, Jeffrey S McAllister wrote:

Microsoft's ethics are important because Microsoft is redefining the world. As most computers run MS products, and more and more computers are being installed into more and more aspects of life, Microsoft's philosophy is magnified. The computer domain can be considered a "space" (a new contintent for exploration). As the "shape" of this space is defined by the tools used to access it, it is possible to view the expansion of applications as the colonization of a new world. This world is an aspect of everyday life. Businesses run on it. Schools teach with it. America fuels its dreams of creating a future where the US will remain economically dominant indefinitely with the growth of the computer industry. We are building a "Brave New World". And the structure set in place will determine how the future starts, and how it will grow. Ethics have been progressively given up to gain power. And as popular perception of ethics seems to redefine itself (instead of being objectively good or bad) the focus on money doesn't seem like such a bad idea. Money has become the quantitative measure of morality. Money defines morality. Gates would certainly argue that all of the questionable tactics his company uses, such as the buyout of competitors and the assimilation of entire new computer industries so they will no longer pose a threat, are merely what is necessary for survival in the modern market economy.

If Microsoft didn't do it, then Microsoft would lose its dominance (and the benefits of standardization.) As Microsoft has the power, Microsoft can define the rules. And the American public doesn't seem to mind too much. People like MS products -- they offer a lot of functionality for a relatively low price, and components which work together (like MS office) truely do make a lot of tasks easier to do. So even though Microsoft violates the spirit of many of the ethics codes (such as these examples from Gotterbarn), the buyers don't care. And as this is a market economy, profits will be the final arbiter in any type of ethical dispute. Microsoft defintely puts its own self-interest ahead of any other concern, in violation of Gotterbarn's 2.06. Not put self-interest, the interest of an employer, the interest of a client, or the interest of the user ahead of the public's interest.

The public would of course benefit from better competition, which would allow superior products access to the market. Microsoft specifically plans its strategy to block such superior products. In the case of JAVA, for instance, a product has surfaced which could, if developed further, allow implementation-independant applications to be built. This would get around many of the limitations of modern computing, and if it were fully developed could make a far greater level of standardization of the industry possible. However, Microsoft is using its influence to split JAVA so that instructions which will work on Internet Explorer will not work on other browsers or on other types of machines. The monopolistic nature of Microsoft "locks in" technology at the current level and obliterates attempts to move to the next level. Of course this makes Microsoft even richer and more dominant, so they are able to sell more copies of their software, so they can become more dominant... The cycle continues. But a lot of people are making money (and the people wishing > to change things aren't) so it will be a while before this cycle stops.

By doing business in this way, Microsoft is defining the rules for future business practices in the software industry and locking in their version of "economic might makes right" morality. But really, in light of the rules of modern business, what kind of company could survive if it wasn't self-centered and profit-centered? A capitalist economy is built on greed. An industrialist economy is built on increasing consumption. There is a coherence here -- one feeds the other. And this will only increase as the world moves to the next phase of the industrial revolution which is the software revolution. Gotterbarn's code 2.06 seems central to all of the other codes. The willingness to hold to any ethical standard means that the ethical standard is more imporant than profits, that honor is more important than immediate gain. And this is what we expect of individuals and corporations in the viscious world of business?

This kind of code is naieve at best, because all anyone can hope to do with such a thing is define appearances. This may be a directive to Bill to stop making it so obvious that his company is putting its own self-interest first, so he may use the resources and media spotlight available to him to project the image of a kinder, gentler Microsoft. But probably not, because this would only be necessary if he thought any profits depended on it. And of course they don't, because unless there is a fundamental restructuring of how people think about the work they do and the business they transact, people can talk all day about ethics and still buy the least expensive products produced with questionable practices. These codes are only a first step -- a reminder that there are some serious, definable problems with ethics in the software world.

The environment will not change until people start believing in ethics, until people are willing to experience pain for their ideals, until they are able to make the *ULTIMATE* sacrifice and back up their ideals with their wallets. But of course it is probably too little too late. Now that the ethical dilemma is so glaringly obvious, the trends of the industrialist, capitalist world have become so entrenched that only a moral revolution of some kind could reverse the momentum towards profits and production and turn the focus toward building a more moral world.
