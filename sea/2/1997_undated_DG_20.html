Here is my response to Notkin Email from Gotterbarn: "Here is my response to Notkin" - 1997? (Level 2) E-mail

Hi Team- Here is my response to Notkin- Comments and corrrections appreciated.
I will sent you the results of the survey on Wednesday.

Dear David,
Thank you for your careful evaluation of the draft Software Engineering Code of Ethics v3.0. I am sorry for the delay in getting back to you but I wanted to clear my response to your concerns with the rest of the committee that has been working on the Code.

We are getting responses from the publication of the Code and survey in the Communications of the ACM and in Computer, so some of our responses to your coments incorporate responses to other comments on the Code.

You are right that we did not specifically mention maintenance in the Code because the committee viewed maintenance as another type of development, another way in which systems evolve. We believe modification of software requires the same care and responsibility as does the original development of the software. As you know, many forms of maintenance involve the development of new subsystems and significant regression testing. We are also aware of the unfortunate circumstance you alluded to where some developers treat maintenance as a mindless exercise. We don't want this to be a standard advocated in the Code. A Code needs to be aspirational if we are to achieve a minimum acceptable standard from all professionals and it will also differentiate professionals from opportunists.

In our effort to emphasize the importance of maintenance by applying the same standards of professional responsibility to maintenance as new development we failed to explicitly mention the relation. We are adding a specific statement that will make clear that the term “development” for the software engineer includes new development, enhancement of existing systems and correction of existing system.
HELP GUYS!!
TARGET SENTENCE. WHAT DO YOU THINK AND WHERE CAN WE PUT SOMETHING LIKE THIS?
“Software engineering is unique in the amount of time spent in maintaining and modifying existing software. This the flexibility and maliability of software systems places special burdens on the software engineer to treat software modification with the same professionalism as new development.”

In case this statement is missed, we have specifically added references to maintenance in the following places:
In the Preamble:
"Those who contribute, by direct participation or by teaching, to the design, development, and maintenance of software systems have significant opportunities both to do good and to cause harm and to influence and enable others to do good or cause harm. ".

" To assure, as much as possible, that this power will be used for good, software engineers must commit themselves to making the design, development and maintenance of software a beneficial, and respected profession. In accordance with that commitment, software engineers shall adhere to the following Code of ethics."

In Principle 8
"8.01. Further their knowledge of developments in the design, development, maintenance and testing of software and related documents, together with the management of the development process"

Even under pressure, the professional doing maintenance must: understand the needed change, design the change, implement and test the change and make sure that the change had no unexpected side effects.

You are right that sometimes maintenance is treated as second class development and people take the dangerous step of modifying software that they do not understand. One of the functions of a Code of ethics is to provide standards of practice that people should aspire toward, so we did not feel that the “second class development” approach to software maintenance should be supported or advocated in a code of good practice.

Your second comment about “shrink wrapped software”development for the market, rather than development for a specific client was right on the mark. The development process is different here because the developer him/herself becomes the client. We are going to stipulate a "client" for shrink wrapped software: the person or persons who must be satisfied before the product is shipped. There is a concern that a shrink wrapped client may agree to software that is damaging in some way to some users in the community. Those potentially effected by this client’s acceptance need to be informed. Development of systems with risks are unavoidable and acceptable, but only when the affected parties are able to choose that risk. Disclaimers on such software would be required. Contractual bargains must be explicit. This also addresses your third point about those who “choose a niche in which their immediate goal is to produce a software product that is likely to be low quality but first on the market “ It is true that many companies try to keep these levels of risk and low quality a secret. But this behavior is viewed as unprofessional. Doctors perform operations which pose different degrees of threat to their patients but the doctor must reveal the level of risk and secure informed consent. “This was the point of 1.14. Promote maximum quality and minimum cost to the employer, the client, the user and the public. Make any tradeoffs clear to all parties concerned.” We do understand that in any software development there will be trade-offs; but the professional is obliged to make these clear. Look at the reactions to the Intel Pentium fiasco.

The next two points are straightforward to fix.
Thank you. Point 4 is right on the mark. 6.15 sharing "useful software?related knowledge, inventions, or discoveries with the profession" is in need of clarification.. A qualification of 6.15 is needed respecting rights of the third parties and confidentiality. We are also adding a caveat about not violating or causing loss of trade secrets, competitive disadvantage etc. This will also be cross referenced to 4.05

Point 5 asks in regard to 1.15 “Are there reasonable and sufficient industry standards that apply to most or all areas of software engineering? “ We struggled with this. We know there are different standards for data processing and real time systems. Unfortunately some developers generalize from the existence of different standards to “there are NO standards". We tried to cover the fact that there are different standards with the word ‘ appropriate’ in 1.15. One of the things that make standards appropriate is the domain of application, shrink wrap software, real-time etc. There is also the problem that there may be no best standard for a particular domain.
1.15 “ Work to follow industry standards that are most appropriate for the task at hand, departing from these only when technically justified.”

will be changed to

1.15 “Work to follow SUFFICIENT industry standards WHEN AVAILABLE that are most appropriate for the task at hand,...”

We hope that “sufficient” implies that there are several acceptable methodologies and that “when available” allows for the fact that for certain projects there may be no best method.


Point 6 refers to the conflict of interest clause in 3.07. Your concern with the absolute prohibitions of 3.07 was shared by others who commented on the Code. People who commented on that clause also had concern that restricting the statement to "financial interest" likely misses other important "interests." The intent of the conflict of interest clause was that potential conflicts of interest be revealed and addressed in ways acceptable to the customer. So we have deleted 3.07. The intent of 3.07 is contained in 3.06 in a way which one avoids undisclosed potential conflicts. The point is to include something about divulging potential conflicts of interest and seeking agreement from affected parties and that this is ok prior to the decision making. If not then they have to cease their involvement. I think it is a case of transparency. Nevertheless, 3.07 is gone.

Your last comment was about 6.07 which says to "only accept remuneration appropriate to professional qualifications or experience." The intent of this was to eliminate implicit bribes. It would be better stated as "never take inflated wages when you know it is a bribe."

Thank you for your comments they have helped to improve the Code. We received very few comments from the publication of an earlier version in SIGSoft this summer.

Our committee is meeting in early December to put the finishing touches on the Code. If you have any further comments we would appreciate seeing them.

Sincerely,
Don Gotterbarn
