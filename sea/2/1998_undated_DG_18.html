Rationale for Software Engineering Coordinating Committee (SWECC)

Dennis J. Frailey and Leonard Tripp
Purpose and Motivation


The purpose of the SWECC is to foster and maintain software engineering as a professional discipline, as a representative of the ACM and the IEEE Computer Society. SWECC is a natural follow-on to the Joint Committee to Establish Software Engineering as a Profession, which was established by the ACM and the IEEE Computer Society in late 1993. The Joint Committee served as a coordinating and focusing mechanism for many ongoing activities in defining software engineering, produced a code of ethics for software engineering, and did preliminary work in defining the body of knowledge and educational recommendations. The SWECC is designed to take this activity into a deployment phase and is prompted by several recent events:
· The ABET has now recognized software engineering as an accreditable form of engineering.
· The Texas Board of Professional Engineers has recognized software engineering as a licensable form of engineering.
· The Texas Board has formally requested that the ACM and the Computer Society, as the primary professional societies representing practicing software engineers, define the body of knowledge on which licensing examinations will be based and sponsor those examinations.
· There is a need to establish a mechanism for accrediting software engineering programs. This will likely receive greater attention after the pending integration of ABET and CSAB.
Approach
The SWECC will achieve its ends through coordination activities at the coordinating committee level and via well defined and individually funded projects at a subcommittee level. Initial projects include finalizing the code of ethics, producing a guide to the body of knowledge, defining performance norms for software engineering tasks (a prerequisite for licensing examinations), and an education project to define model curricula and recommend assessment criteria. The SWECC will represent the two societies and, through them, practicing software engineers as it carries out these and later projects. The societies will have equal representation on SWECC and on SWECC projects.
Major projects, such as the body of knowledge and performance norms projects, will be funded primarily by soliciting membership from industry and government entities on project advisory boards. Affected elements of the societies may also be asked to become members of such advisory boards and to provide appropriate shares of the funding. Response from prospective members of the body of knowledge advisory board have been encouraging, with several commitments and others pending.

Relationship to Societies

SWECC will be a wholly and jointly owned entity of the ACM and the IEEE Computer Society and will consist of three representatives from each society. The initial chair of the SWECC will be one of the Computer Society representatives and the initial vice chair will be one of the ACM representatives. The chair and vice chair will alternate on a two year rotating basis. Representatives may have terms chosen by their respective societies, but a three year term is suggested. Each society’s representatives will report to their respective society as determined appropriate to the society (once per year is suggested).

Projects of the SWECC will generally be carried out as joint efforts of the societies with SWECC serving to oversee and coordinate these projects for overall consistency and for the benefit of the software engineering profession. Major projects will be funded independently of SWECC, although the societies may contribute to such funding.
