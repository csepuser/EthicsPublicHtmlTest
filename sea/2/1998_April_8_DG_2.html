Request that the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice (version 4) be considered Email from Gotterbarn to Charles House and Frailey: "Request that the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice (version 4) be considered" - April 8, 1998 (Level 2) E-mail

8 April 1998

Dear Charles House and Dennis Frailey:

As chair of the IEEE-CS/ACM Joint Task Force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practice (SEEPP), I respectfully request that the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice (version 4) be considered at the next Executive Council meeting in May for adoption by the ACM. A similar request is being made to the President of the IEEE-CS for adoption by their board of governors at their June meeting.

The SEEPP task force sent the Code and their letter of recommendation to the Joint Steering Committee in December and a follow up letter in January.(See attachments below). We understand the Steering Committee's response has been delayed, owing to a reorganization. This delay has several unfortunate consequences.

The Code is the only finished product so far of the IEEE-CS/ACM's joint effort to professionalize software engineering. The computing community has criticized this joint effort as going nowhere. Not approving the Code only contributes to this unfortunate view of this important joint effort.

At a recent software engineering meeting where the process of licensing software engineers in Texas was being discussed, the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice was described as a significant contribution to professionalization and licensing of software engineers. It would be unfortunate if the Texas Board adopted this Code as their standard before it was approved by its sponsoring societies.

The development of the Code was an international process and it has already received international recognition. This recognition includes the Code's adoption as a code of practice by industry such as Siemen's Software Development, India. It is being published in several computer ethics text books--including Kevin Bowyer's revision of his IEEE-CS book on Computer Ethics-- and the Code has been discussed at several professional meetings. It is also forthcoming in the Journal of Business Ethics, Journal of Science and Engineering Ethics, the Proceeding of the ACM Policy Conference in May and the SIGCSE Bulletin.

As indicated in our original request to the Steering Committee, the Code has undergone extensive review by those who would be affected by its adoption. ACM and IEEE-CS members throughout the world have seen its publication in Computer and CACM. Comments have been solicited over several web sites, and from professional programmers by managers from industry. The Code has been reviewed by ethics officers of large industries, philosophers, and ethicists. The resulting document represents a general consensus of the moral obligations of individual software engineers and the profession.

I hope you will agree that the ACM and the IEEE-CS will need to finish this project by approving this Code. Such approval is consistent with the original goals of the Joint Steering Committee and will help define and direct the profession. The absence of such approval indicates a lack of support for ethical practices. A continuing failure to endorse the Code will force each software practitioner to re-invent the morality of software engineering.

The Code addresses both the responsibility of the practicing professional and of the profession. We believe that the societies representing the profession need to take a stand to provide all software engineers with an ethical direction and with a tool they can use to educate others -- including their management-- about the ethical responsibilities of the software engineer. The approval of this Code will clearly state the moral commitments of practicing professionals and of the profession.

Please let us know what can do to forward this process.

Thank you very much for your attention.

Sincerely, The Executive Committee of the SEEPP Task Force

Donald Gotterbarn-Director, Software Engineering Ethics Research Institute Keith Miller- ACM SIGS Chair, and Simon Rogerson-Director, Centre For Computing and Social Responsibility

=============Original Letters below===========

19 January 1998 Dear Steering Committee for the Professionalization of Software Engineering:

A month ago I sent a recommendation from our task force for the approval of the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice by the Steering Committee and its recommendation to the Board of Governors of the IEEE-CS and the Executive Committee of the ACM. I have not heard any response to this request.

I believe the Board of Governors of the IEEE-CS and the Executive Committee of the ACM have meetings scheduled in February and I would be willing to represent the SEEPP task force at these meetings. It would be salubrious if the Code were approved at those meetings. There are already several events scheduled for February and March which include the Code. Articles about the Code ,for example, are forthcoming in CACM Risks Column, the SIGCAS Newsletter, and the Lockheed Martin newsletter. The Code will be used in a Master Class at Ethicomp'98 (Rotterdam), in a workshop at the Conference on Software Engineering Education and Training sponsored by the Computer Society and the Software Engineering Institute(Atlanta). The Code is also the subject of a paper at the ACM Policy Conference(Washington, DC) and a presentation to industrialist in Australia. These meetings related to the Code would also provide a venue for the leadership of the Societies to announce their support of this ethical standard and mark the achievements of the Joint Steering Committee.

The purpose of the task force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices is to document the ethical and professional responsibilities and obligations of software engineers. The task Force developed a code for a sub-specialization within the constituencies of both organizations and the profession itself.

But the Code is much more than that. The goal of the IEEE-CS/ACM Steering Committee was to Professionalize Software Engineering. Software engineering as a discipline has particular idiosyncratic needs, as described in the details of the Code. The success of this effort to articulate the professional responsibilities of software engineers has already been recognized. The Texas Board of Professional Engineer's Licensing Committee, in a recent meeting that addressed the subject of professional registration of software engineers, recognized software engineering as a new discipline. Specifically as a disciple with its own foundations and unique body of knowledge. They also expressed high regard for the Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice. Specificity of practice is the key to the elaborated Code..

The professionalization of software engineering requires a Code which is international and can be adopted by professional organizations, industry, and individual professionals. We have developed a Code that straddles the ACM/IEEE-CS gulf, and differs enough from both the ACM and IEEE's more general codes to attract attention from non-organizational types (like industries). From the evidence, we seem to have accomplished both of these goals. The Code has received support from numerous countries including Australia, Canada, Czechoslovakia, Egypt, Germany, India, Ireland, Netherlands, United Kingdom, United States, and Uruguay. This and other evidence from our survey indicates that the Code has international consensus. The Code also has received support from numerous industries, from large multi-nationals to small software development firms.

The Code can function as an ethical charter for the profession. Its joint support by the ACM and the IEEE-CS is a good idea, it shows the public that different organizations within the same industry can cooperate, and makes it easier for professionals to understand what their obligations are. If there are any questions please feel free to contact me. I look forward to working with the Steering Committee to bring this task to a successful completion.

Sincerely, Donald Gotterbarn for the SEEPP Task Force.

Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice-http://www.acm.org/serving

===================Original Letter======================= December 16, 1997

To the Joint IEEE-CS/ACM Steering Committee on the Professionalization of Software Engineering and Executive Committees of the ACM and the IEEE-CS:

The Task Force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices has completed its work in developing a Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice and recommends its approval by both professional societies. We recommend this action for the following reasons: Support by both societies will be a public assertion by the profession of a unified commitment to ethical responsibility, The Code represents an international standard of practice, The Code has been reviewed and adopted by industry, The Code presents a broad consensus of the profession, and There is wide demonstrated positive support, including from Academia, for the Code.

This Code complements the more general codes of ethics of both societies which address the issues of the computing profession as a whole. The Code of Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practice addresses the ethical issues and standards of conduct which specifically arise within the practice of software engineering. In this sense the code is not a replacement for any existing code.

The approval of this Code by both societies will be a significant forward step for the software engineering profession. The cooperative support given by both societies to the development and approval of this Code underscores the importance of a commitment to ethical practices by the profession and will serve as a model for other developing professions.

The development of this Code was unique in its international flavor. The task force and the executive committee had international representation, including from Europe, Africa, Australia, and North America. As the process of development and revision proceeded, this network of international participation and comment spread throughout the world. In an effort to be consistent with the codes of the major computing professional societies throughout the world, the task force solicited responses from members of the major computing societies. Comment and support was also received from the International Federation of Information Processing (IFIP). The task force also included two lawyers specializing in computer law, and one representative from the military.

The Code has been positively reviewed by several ethics officers and ethics directors of multinational organizations after sharing it with software engineers in their organizations. Such industries included: Lockheed Martin, Northrup Gruman, and Digital Equipment. We also heard from CEO's of smaller companies indicating strong support for the Code. In fact, the Code has already, in its preliminary form, been adapted by St.Paul Companies as their information systems code of ethics with only minor modifications, such as, replacing the expression "software engineer" with "IS professional".

The Code was developed using a process of continuous review and revision. The final draft of the Code, together with a questionnaire, was published on several web sites, in some ACM SIG journals and in Computer and CACM. The results of the survey were that most items had greater than 95 percent support with many at 100 percent. The highest level of opposition to any clause was 12%. In the light of feedback, the Principles and associated Clauses have been reviewed and modified where necessary. We are confident that if the survey were to be taken again, all Clauses would receive an approval rating of over 95% The Code has also received a positive response from academe. We have received several requests to have the Code included in computer ethics and software engineering textbooks and have been asked to develop case studies for software engineering classes based on the Code.

The Code has already proven to be a dynamic and useful document becoming a model within at least one industry. Both societies have already recognized that education about their codes of ethics is an important issue. Several members of the SEEPP task force are on the IEEE-CS and the ACM committees which are responsible for the maintenance of their codes and education of the membership about their codes of ethics. To support the maintenance of and education about the Code, we plan to maintain it initially on the Software Engineering Ethics Research Institute's and Centre for Computing and Social Responsibility's web sites. The SEEPP Task Force will continue to gather input from the community about the Code and develop and share examples of using the Code. Industry has already donated case studies related to the Clauses of the Code. It would be beneficial to the profession if both societies endorsed this effort and approved the Code.

Sincerely, The Executive Committee of the SEEP Task Force

Donald Gotterbarn-Director, Software Engineering Ethics Research Institute Keith Miller- ACM SIGS Chair, and Simon Rogerson-Director, Centre For Computing and Social Responsibility
