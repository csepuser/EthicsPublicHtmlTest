Contracting Software for Government Acquisition Systems and Potential Ethical Concerns

written by CAPTAIN JONAS VOGELHUT
December 9, 1998

Introduction Contracting Conclusions Background Types of Contracts References Computer Ethics Issues Enclosures Software Acquisition Recommendations Introduction Observing the direction in Military procurement, the purchase of software, both embedded into weapons systems and as stand alone products, is a large $268 billion dollar part of the Department of Defense's acquisition strategy. This reliance on software evolved as technological advances in the software industry allowed for the creation of products that can do more (process more memory, work faster, aim a missile straighter, etc.). The government relies on software to meet the needs of the soldier for more capable systems, yet there is a general lack of understand the capabilities of software, and how to manage the procurement. As the software industry has seen rapid expansion, the parallel development of ethical standards of conduct for software engineers (those that write the code) has not matured as quickly. These two factors create a dilemma for the contracting of that software, since the limited understanding by the user and the lack of industry standards increase the risk that software acquisitions will not meet the user requirements.

The purpose of my paper is to research how the Department of Defense can improve the acquisition of software applications and embedded software, and ensure ethical practices in the software engineers. First, I will begin with an overview of what are ethics, and how the computer industry defines ethics in the development of software. Next, I will review how the Department of Defense (DoD) acquires software, and the problems that arise. Lastly, I shall examine the issues in the development of software, and discuss what actions the Government can take to ensure the acquisition of the best software products for the user.

Top Background To understand ethical practices in software engineering and contract, it is important to establish what are ethics and how they apply to the computer industry. Webster's revised unabridged dictionary (1913) defines ethics as: "The science of human duty; the body of rules of duty drawn from this science; a particular system of principles and rules concerting duty, whether true or false; rules of practice in respect to a single class of human actions; as, political or social ethics; medical ethics." I understand ethics as a combination of two factors. First, ethics is a study of morals. The personal decision of what is right and what is wrong. Second, ethics is a standard for behavior of a particular group, such as Business Ethics or Medical Ethics. In a purchase transaction, there are ethical standards for the buyer and the seller. As a buyer, FAR part 3.101 requires that "Government business shall be conducted in a manner above reproach…." I understand this and other factors to mean that the Department of Defense (military and civilian) personnel are held to the higher ethical standard than other professions, for two reasons.

First, those professionals are morally obligated to follow the orders of their superiors or be courageous in the face of physical danger (both will go into combat zones) [Ref 24]. Second, government employees, especially the military, are more in the public spotlight that other workers, and while being scrutinized, they are bound more strictly to the moral standards that apply to everyone else. Although these explanations show the need for high ethical standard for government officials, what ethical standards should DoD expect in the defense industry? First, DFARS part 203.7000 cites that "Government contractors must conduct themselves with the highest degree of honesty and integrity." The DFARS goes on to state that the contractor's management controls provide for a written ethical code, a reporting mechanism, and a disciplinary system for improper conduct. Second, I believe we should expect that the work produced by defense contractors represent the ethics and ideals of the personnel who use the products or benefit from the services. Few in society will blame the contractor when the artillery shell lands outside the engagement area and into the commissary parking lot, yet the round may overshoot due to a faulty trajectory plan as determined by the on-board computer. The newspaper headlines might state that the howitzer is to blame and is unsafe to use, but focus of condemnation will be on the military (why did we buy such an unsafe weapon?).

The ethical practices in our contractors must start with a clear statement of the standards that govern the conduct of all employees in their relationships to the industry. This should be followed up with specific ethical policies for the specific company, in its dealings with customers, suppliers, competitors, and oversight personnel. This statement also must state the consequences of violating those standards, and the responsibility of management for monitoring and enforcing the standards. [Ref 13]

What are computer ethics? Before understanding the standards of ethics in software engineering and the contracting for software, it is important to establish a baseline of what ethics standards are currently in place (as published by the industry). In a more broad definition, "Computer ethics is generally presented as any misuse of computer technology, from stock fraud to using the word processor to make Christmas letters, or it is portrayed as any significant social issue involving computers." [Ref 28] Unfortunately, this is too general a definition to explain of the relationship between the government and a software contractor for the writing of new software products. Computer ethics deals with the actions a user partakes while using a computer, such as while surfing the Internet, or when it is permissible to use the office computer to play solitaire. In my attempt to narrow the definition to software ethics, I found an unanticipated lack of published ethical standards.

Dr. Donald Gotterbarn, Professor at East Tennessee State Universities School for Ethics commented that, "[This] absence of published ethical standards is surprising because industry perceives a need for training in software engineering ethics. The significance industry gives to this is evident by the place of ethics officers in corporations, e.g., the ethics officers of Boeing Computer Services, Raytheon, and Texas Instruments are all CEOs at the vice presidential level." [Ref 28] In my attempt to research Dr. Gotternbarn's assertion, I interviewed three professors from the Naval Postgraduate School concerning the amount of ethics training given to graduate students working toward their master's degree in software engineering. All three professors came to the same conclusion. Not only does the Naval Postgraduate School not specifically teach ethics courses to the students in software engineering, but also there is no perceived need for the training. There are lecture blocks on computer ethics, since the Computer Science Accreditation Board requires some discussion of ethics in the curriculum for the school to be accredited. However, there are no discussions on ethical actions during the writing of code. This overall lack of training in software ethics downplays its importance, and leaves open the possibility of violations.

I located very few discussions referring to ethics training for Software Engineers, and those I did find generally centered specifically on the shortages of such training. I envision this omission of ethical training in the education of software engineers is caused by a mindset in the industry that is focused with defining the technical standards of software engineering, and not enforcing the ethical actions of the engineer. Even the best available definitions for software ethics subdivide the topic into the technical merits (like business ethics) and the professional ethics (for decision making), instead of what actions or mindsets a software engineer should have.

The Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) and the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers, Inc. (IEEE) have both published standards for ethics in the computer industry, but these standards are not as valuable for the software engineer, since they focus on general engineering and computer respectively, and not the combination of both. (See Enclosure 1). In short, although the computer industry beliefs are that ethics in general is important, the writing of quality code is more important. I believe this is a signal for potential danger, since the software industry is a booming industry, yet the focus of the workers and leadership is on product (working software) and not the process (how did we make it work).

Software Acquisition The DoD has many approaches to choose from in the construction of an acquisition strategy. According to DoD 5000.2, three typical models include the traditional model, the grand design model, and the incremental model. The traditional model uses a structure of Concept Exploration, Program Development and Risk Reduction, Engineering and Manufacturing Development, and Production/Fielding. The grand design model combines concept, development, and fielding into a single step. The incremental model phases the steps of acquisition, and is used when requirements are well understood, but other considerations (risks, funding, schedule, etc.) require more time to develop. Software acquisition can use any of these models in a similar fashion to hardware programs. One difference is that the product you receive is not an item you can hold or a service you can observe, but a computer program or an internal mechanism for a piece of hardware. Since the choice of model depends on maturity of requirements and time available, stand-alone software acquisitions generally use the traditional model, where embedded software follows the strategy of the associated hardware product. Another difference is in testing, where stand-alone software testing may be less physical yet but more interactive with the intelligence of the user, and hardware testing will demonstrate the capabilities of the hardware system.

Contracting The difficulty in software acquisition is founded in the requirements analysis and the lack of understanding of costs, especially during contract administration. This is true for three reasons. First, the definitions of user needs/requirements change with technology. Either the user defines requirements based on current technology, which can be obsolete before equipment is fielded, or the requirements are defined based on projections of technology, which may not be met. Second, the majority of cost in a software program is not in the initial writing of code, but in the debugging of the code (similar to multiple attempts at developmental testing an item of hardware). Finally, the software industry has a shortage of software engineers, so those available engineers are sought by the best paying (and most expensive companies). Realizing the difficulties in software acquisition, the Defense Contract Management Center (DCMC) established a ten-step process for software specialists to assist the contracting officer in the acquisition of software. [Ref 12] Although some consideration is given to pre-award activities, the focus is contract administration. The highlights from this list include:

1. Specifications and Standards. Review the specifications and standards included in the RFP, identify potential risk areas, and provide focus on areas to emphasize. More work done before contract award leads to less problems after award.

2. Review documentation and resources. Items such as the contract, the Statement of Work (SOW), and the software specific paperwork, (Software Development Plan (SDP), Software Quality Assurance (SQA) Plan, etc.) help in the identification of requirements, schedules, perform planning, and possible high risk in the areas of technical, schedule, quality and cost. Resources, including Government Furnished Equipment and firmware must be adequate to develop and test the software.

3. Verify proper provisions for Government rights to the data produced under contract. Most commercial off the shelf software products contain clauses that retain all data rights, and restrict usage of the software, such as to one machine. Software that would be required on multiple systems (such as a stockpile of missile guidance systems) requires more freedom to the data.

4. Identify, discuss, and attempt to resolve contract deficiencies. The longer a problem goes unresolved and the contractor makes the action a routine, the less probability to change the behavior later.

5. Participate In A Post-Award Orientation Conference (PAOC): Identify misunderstandings, conflicts or misinterpretations in the contract. PAOC's are very useful for contractors who have never performed computer software development under a government contract, or if the software development plans do not adequately describe how work will be performed.

6. Develop A Surveillance Plan that supplements the Contract Administration Office (CAO) plan to monitor the contractor’s development of software. Identify surveillance activities such as requirements traceability, metrics to be used, processes to be assessed, product audits and milestone dates. Highlight areas of cost, schedule, quality and technical performance risk that would suggest a need for increased surveillance. (See Enclosure 2 for the Software Development Surveillance Flowchart). The Naval Command, Control, and Ocean Surveillance Center's Research, Development, Test and Evaluation Division (NRaD) has also developed a flow diagram to assist Project (Program) Managers identify the scope of activity needed in the procurement and oversight of software contracts. It helps to document the processes that all software project managers use in planning, working to develop, and maintaining software, all in an effort to establish best practices and lessons learned. (See Enclosure 3).

Initial goals that NRaD established for Program Managers includes [Ref 42]:

1. Selection of qualified contractors. Past performance must be a factor in the source selection evaluation.

2. Understanding of standards, procedures, and requirements written into the contract by both the Government and the Contractor. The clearer the requirements in the contract are to both side, the less appeals and legal actions later in the program.

3. Communications throughout the lifecycle of the contract. Having the contractor, a software engineer, and a government representative on an integrated product team will help identify and solve potential conflicts as they arise.

4. Governmental tracking of contractor's actual results and performance against contract requirements. This will lead to a better visualization of potential shortfalls and reduce the risk of breaking cost, schedule, and performance baselines.

Types of Contracts The criteria for software contract selection are similar to those for hardware systems. FAR part 16 describes that contract types be determined by the Government to make the contractor's cost responsibility proportionate with the risk required to perform. For software, when requirements are well defined (such as for software upgrades) and the risk of development is low, a fixed-price contract is used. When requirements are less well understood and development risk is high, a more flexible cost-reimbursable contract is the better choice. Indefinite delivery contracts are used for software support efforts, since maintena nce requirements, scheduling, and the availability of funds may be later defined based on needs from the user. [Ref 42] In practice, actual contracts used on systems vary between the services and commands, although the trend is toward cost contracts due to the risks. According to David Zentner of the DCMC Software Center, "When large new software intensive systems are procured, the Government generally chooses to go with Cost Reimbursable contracts, to provide for sharing of the risk between the Government and the contractor."

Issues I believe there are two fundamental issues in software procurement for the federal government. First, the government gets overcharged (in life cycle costs) for software because we do not have enough experience or expertise in the area to understand what is being procured (analysis of cost and pricing data). These leads to contract awards being based solely on the lowest priced software proposal, and not a combination of life cycle costs, scheduled deliveries, and performance factors. Eventually, the government is forced to buy maintenance agreements or software upgrades from the contractors that add to the initial purchase, usually at substantially higher prices. Second, that the government buys software that does not meet long-term needs, or is not mature enough to meet operational testing requirements (This stems from the users request that software solve all problems that cannot be fixed by additional hardware, regardless of the technical feasibility.) My beliefs fall in line with the OSD "Improving Software Acquisition Management" workshops top five problem areas in software acquisition [Ref 38], namely, · Requirements - Often too vague and often expand mid-acquisition. · Mismatch between "acquirer" and "supplier" maturity - Either the contractor or the government does not understand the requirement enough to build the correct product. · Short term vs. long term perspective and expectations - Is the software a short term fix to a crisis problem (so requirements for continuous upgrades is acceptable [example = Patriot Missile]), or is this software a long term product (such as the Global Command and Control System). · Resources to implement - Does the program office have the time, money, people, and training to understand and supervise the acquisition? · Organizational commitment to change ("Corps" down to "team") - Sharing of information between contractor and government in an alliance will result in better systems. For ethical issues, the dilemma for a contractor falls between factors of cost, schedule, and performance. Systems fielded on time and at cost may not be sufficiently tested against contingencies that the programmer expects to occur, but if those tests are not specified in the contract (such as system overloads), it is not a requirement (hence the contractor does not have to do it, but the question is should he do it). The system that performs with all possible glitches fixed (without potential system failures) often will be above cost and schedule objectives. This will lead to unhappy government administrators (which may lead to loss of future contract after being labeled a poor performer.) As an example of this, I will use an equipment tracking program. This acquisition system will be tested for use with all equipment specifically listed in the contract. I will assume that it has passed all developmental and operational tests. After passing these milestones, it still may not, however, have other desirable capabilities, such as the flexibility to add in future weapons systems, or handle Joint/Combined equipment tracking (which was not a requirement, but simple to write into the program, and a reasonable follow on stage for the software). The contractor must choose between releasing the product and continuing to work on upgrades while it is being used, or failing to release the product, possibly defaulting on the contract, but maintaining that the system was not ready for the user actual needs (actual versus stated needs). At worst case, people can die due to faulty software. This software usually performed its routine tasks well, and therefore passes testing and is bought/ fielded. Unfortunately for the user, poor design or debugging against predictable situation can lead to the loss of lives.

One example of this is: "In 1992, the London Ambulance Service placed into operation a new computerized dispatching system without any organized backup procedure. When the system overloaded, the operator had no ability to dispatch calls manually. Some callers were put on hold for up to 30 minutes. One disabled woman was trapped in her chair by the body of her collapsed husband. By the time an ambulance finally arrived, her husband had died. [Ref 26]" I do not believe however that the government or the defense contractors for the government face this type of ethical issue. Most of my research indicates a true desire on the part of the government and the software contractor to field the highest level of performance software. The concern is not will the system work, but will the system need upgrades, and how much will upgrades and other maintenance actions cost. Ted Lewis, Lecturer at the Naval Postgraduate School, explained the issue this way: "The government gets snookered. We get locked into a desire for the latest technology at the lowest cost. When the cost is lowest up front, we pay more to fix [the bugs] later." [Ref 36] The government has difficulty in effectively pricing the costs within software development programs, since attempts at estimating the engineer labor hours required are only partially successful at best. Most of the time required to write a program is not in the initial writing of code, but in the debug process. The reuse of code concept can save money in the initial writing of code, but this process often takes longer to debug (unless the original code was written with reuse in the plan). A software surveillance representative has to truly understand the intricacies of software engineering in order to know when a software program is requiring more man- hours than expected. A rule of thumb for industry expects new code development at 5-12 lines per week, and 12-20 lines per week for reused code [Ref 51]. These are only gauges, but are methods for estimations of price. This same calculation should be used as an evaluation technique of the price estimates in contract proposals (to check for price realism).

As most contracts are written as cost reimbursable contracts (priced by man-hours required [Ref 51]), the longer it takes to write and debug a program, the more money a contractor will receive. One tool available for cost estimation is called COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model, versions 81 and II). This program calculates software costs based on the number of source lines of code, the use of the program (stand-alone software or embedded), product attributes (reliability), computer attributes (storage, speed), personnel attributes (experience level), and project attributes (schedule). The use of programs like this take some of the variability and unknowns out of the process, but they are still developing in reliability and usefulness to military programs.

Recommendations In my opinion, the best ways the Department of Defense has to avoid software and ethical issues are through effective contract administration (software development surveillance) and better contracting for software. Improvements in contract administration require an emphasis on training both the government and the contractor. Better contracting fo r software can be achieved through the implementation of the best practices of the computer industry. In response to these two situations, and the fact that DCMC was spending 37% of its funding on software with only 7% of its personnel trained on software, [Ref 11] the DoD established the DCMC Software Center. The goal of this center is to establish training for software representatives and capture the best practices and processes from government and industry for use on all other software acquisitions and contracts. Some specific tasks of this center include [Ref 39] · Support source selection, develop a software process repository; interface with the Software Engineering Institute · Provide guidance and technical support on Post Award surveillance activities · Ensure a highly competent software workforce, manages the Software Professional Development Program Level III (senior level) certification process · Provide integrated tool box for surveillance personnel I believe the government must train the administrative contracting officers and the software representatives in software surveillance techniques. Topics should include what warning signs to watch that indicate violations; how to ask questions to gauge the ethical atmosphere; and how to conduct basic reviews of corporate ethical standards. (What are the rules and the punishment for breaking those rules?) Contractors should have internal training programs for ethics, mechanisms to report violations, and well defined disciplinary actions corresponding to those infractions. Effortless reporting ethical reminders are as simple as posting the DoD Hotline poster (for contract awards over $5 million.)

One functional training plan in process today is the Air Force's Software Professional Development Program (SPDP). This program strives to improve general software engineering expertise, by offering a curriculum in software engineering (four courses via distance learning and one laboratory capstone course taught at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base, Ohio). This class is mandatory for entry-level members of the software engineer career field to upgrade and be awarded the fully qualified status. Besides training of personnel, the government can improve its software acquisition through the adoption of the best business practices in industry. (Note: This is the idea embedded in acquisition reform). The Airlie Software Council identified nine principal best practices observed in industry, and deemed them essential for nearly all DoD software development projects. [Ref 43] Some important principles include: 1. Establishment of a formal risk management process, requiring corporate acceptance of risk as a major consideration for software program management. This includes commitment of program resources, and established methods for identifying, monitoring, and managing risk. 3. Conducting inspections on the requirements, architecture, detailed design, code, and test plans. 6. Render the core indicators of project status readily available to all project participants. 9. Make management accountable training as well as for fostering an environment conducive to high morale and low voluntary staff turnover. The desired outcome of the training and best practice use is a higher quality, lower risk program.

Carnegie Mellon University's Software Engineering Institute developed a certification system that incorporates training, use of best practices, and other management factors to rate programs on their maturity and ability to field a quality program. The Software Acquisition Capability Maturity Model (SA-CMM) describes the essential elements of managing and improving the acquisition process in an organization, and outlines a managed path for improving the process for acquiring software. [Ref 3] I would compare the use of this model to the drive in industry toward ISO 9001 and other similar standards. Although ISO has a draft standard for software engineering (ISO 12207), more respect is given to companies with a higher CMM level. The Software Engineering Institute, Carnegie Mellon University estimates that if you contract with a level one company, you can expect to pay $300-660 per line of code, and have 3-9 defects per work unit. When you contract with a level three company, you can expect to pay only $140 per line on code, and have one defect per work unit [Ref 3]. It was interesting to discover that using this graded scale, only three commercial companies and one government agency (The Ogden Air Logistics Center, Software Engineering Division at Hill Air Force Base, Utah) have been assessed as Level 5 maturity organizations to date. The bottom line is that using this model, a contractor better understand the quality and costs associated with a contract action, to reduce some of the unknowns, and ultimately negotiate for a better software product at a fair and reasonable price.

Conclusion Contracting for software is not a simple task, since the state of technology expands so rapidly and the government does not have the expertise to manage the process. DoD has improved its software acquisition ability through the use of training programs and incorporation of the best practices from industry. I do not believe there are problems in the ethical behavior of software engineers involved in government contracts. However, the lack of industry standards and formalized training in ethics for those engineers is a source for future concern. There also is a potential source of ethical violations if the government contracts are overcharging the government for software due to the lack of experts in understanding the costs associated with writing code. To lower the risks for these concerns affecting government procurements, contracting officers must continue their vigilance in contract administration and software surveillance.

The IEEE code of ethics We, the members of the IEEE, in recognition of the importance of our technologies in affecting the quality of life throughout the world, and in accepting a personal obligation to our profession, its members and the communities we serve, do hereby commit ourselves to conduct of the highest ethical and professional manner and agree: to accept responsibility in making engineering decisions consistent with the safety, health, and welfare of the public, and to disclose promptly factors that might endanger the public or the environment; to avoid real or perceived conflicts of interest whenever possible, and to disclose them to affected parties when they do exist; to be honest and realistic in stating claims or estimates based on available data; to reject bribery in all of its forms; to improve understanding of technology; its appropriate application, and potential consequences; to maintain and improve our technical competence and to undertake technological tasks for others only if qualified by training or experience, or after full disclosure of pertinent limitations; to seek, accept, and offer honest criticism of technical work, to acknowledge and correct errors, and to credit properly the contributions of others; to treat fairly all persons regardless of such factors as race, religion, gender, disability, age, or national origin; to avoid injuring others, their property, reputation, or employment by false or malicious action; to assist colleagues and co-workers in their professional development and to sup- port them in following this code of ethics.

The Association for Computing Machinery Code of Ethics (1992)

1. General Moral Imperatives.

As an ACM member I will...
1.1 Contribute to society and human well-being.
1.2 Avoid harm to others.
1.3 Be honest and trustworthy.
1.4 Be fair and take action not to discriminate.
1.5 Honor property rights including copyrights and patents.
1.6 Give proper credit for intellectual property.
1.7 Respect the privacy of others.
1.8 Honor confidentiality.

2. More Specific Professional Responsibilities.

As an ACM computing professional I will...
2.1 Strive to achieve the highest quality in both the process and products of professional work.
2.2 Acquire and maintain professional competence.
2.3 Know and respect existing laws pertaining to professional work.
2.4 Accept and provide appropriate professional review.
2.5 Give comprehensive and thorough evaluations of computer systems and their impacts, including analysis of possible risks.
2.6 Honor contracts, agreements, and assigned responsibilities.
2.7 Improve public understanding of computing and its consequences.
2.8 Access computing and communication resources only when authorized to do so.

3. Organizational Leadership Imperatives.

As an ACM member and an organizational leader, I will...
3.1 Articulate social responsibilities of members of an organizational unit and encourage full acceptance of those responsibilities.
3.2 Manage personnel and resources to design and build information systems that enhance the quality, effectiveness and dignity of working life.
3.3 Acknowledge and support proper and authorized uses of an organization's computing and communications resources.
3.4 Ensure that users and those who will be affected by a computing system have their needs clearly articulated during the assessment and design of requirements. Later the system must be validated to meet requirements.
3.5 Articulate and support policies that protect the dignity of users and others affected by a computing system.
3.6 Create opportunities for members of the organization to learn the principles and limitations of computer systems.

4. Compliance with the Code. As an ACM member, I will...
4.1 Uphold and promote the principles of this code.
4.2 Treat violations of this code as inconsistent with membership in the ACM.
