Recepient: Don Gotterbarn

Source: Don Gotterbarn

Ethical Issues in s/w Engineering

Actually, a move toward software licensing doesn't have to be inevitable. The New Jersey legislature was flooded with letters of protest, and most of the senators that I received responses from said they would be voting against such legislation. I believe that our industry should take a hard line "NO" stance.

What would be the purpose of licensing? I think the prospect of additional revenue (read: tax dollars) is attractive to the states. After all, aren't we software types all overpaid anyway? How would licensing assure a client that they were getting a qualified engineer? Look at other professions. Does hiring "license" mean quality? There are thousands of unqualified and unscrupulous quack lawyers, doctors, contractors, etc. out there -- all licensed. And as your letter said, none of the proposed licensing schemes addressed ethics -- supposedly "the" reason for licensing in the first place!

What skills would have to be demonstrated to become licensed? A broad-based exam would necessarily have to be so general that it would certainly be no indication of someone's skills. Would an exam specific to the type of engineering currently performed mean you'd have to get another license to do a different kind of work?

I think that certification is a better alternative to licensing. You would not have to be "certified" before being allowed to work. But being certified in a specialized field would, minimally, ensure your clients that you've passed a (rigorous) exam by your peers and demonstrated advanced skills in that area.

Don't give up the fight against software licensing!
