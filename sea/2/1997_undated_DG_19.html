Dear David,

Thank you for your carfeul evaluation of the draft Software Engineering Code of Ethics. I am sorry for the delay in getting back to you but I wanted to clear my response to your concerns with the rest of the committee.

We are also getting reponses from the publication of the Code in the Communications of the ACM and in Computer, so some of our responses to your coments incorporate responses to other comments on the code.

You are right that we did not have specific mention of maintenance in the Code but the committee viewed maintance as another type of development, another way in which systems evolve. We believe modification of software requires the same care and responsiblity as does the original development of the software.. As you know, many forms of maintenance invlove the development of new subsystems and significant regression testing. We are also aware of the unfortunate circumstance where some developer treat maintenance as a mindless exercise. A Code need to be aspirational. If we are to achieve a minimum accepatable standard from all professionals and that this will also differentiate professionals from opportunists.

In our effort to emphasize the improtance of maintenance by applying the same standards of professional responsibility as new development we failed to explicitly mention the relation. We are adding a specific statement that will make clear that the term development includes new develpment, enhancement of existing system and correction of existing system. Target sentence. “One of the unique features of SE is maliability of already developed systems. This feature of software systems places special burdens on the software engineer to treat software modification with the same professionalism as new development.”

In case this statement is missed, we have specifically added references to maintenance in the following places: preamble: "Those who contribute, by direct participation or by teaching, to the design, development, and maintenance of software systems have significant opportunities both to do good and to cause harm and to influence and enable others to do good or cause harm. ".

" To assure, as much as possible, that this power will be used for good, software engineers must commit themselves to making the design, development and maintenance of software a beneficial, and respected profession. In accordance with that commitment, software engineers shall adhere to the following Code of ethics."

"8.01. Further their knowledge of developments in the design, development, maintenance and testing of software and related documents, together with the management of the development process"

Even under pressure the professional must understand the needed change, design the change, impelement and test the change and make sure that the change had no unexpected side effects. You are right that sometimes maintanance is treated as second class development and people take the dangerous step of modifying code they do not understand. One of the functions of a code of ethics is to provide standards of practice that people should aspire toward so we did not feel that this approach to software maintenance should be suported or advocated in a code of Good practice.

We have added to bullet 1.01 1.01. Ensure that specifications for software on which they work have been well documented, satisfy the user’s requirements, and have the client’s approval. 1.02 Ensure that not work- maybe insertt into preamble something like Software engineering is unique in that significant is spent developing products for which there is no imediate custon\mer form of engineering in that a large percentage of the development process is spent modifying

Some fixes are mechanical.

Your comment 2) about “shrinkwrapped software”development for the market, rather than development for a specific client was right on the mark. The development process is different here because the developer him/herself becomes the client. We are going to stipulate a "client" for shinkwrapped software: the person or persons who must be satisfied before the product is shipped. There is a concern that a shrinkwrapped client may agree to software that is damaging is some way to some users in the community. Those potnetially effected by this client acceptance need to be nformed. Development of systems with risks are unavoidable and acceptable, but ONLYwhen the affected parties are able to choose that risk. Disclaimers on such software would be required. Contractual bargains must be explicit. This also addresses your thid point about those who “ “choose a niche in which their immediate goal is to produce a software product that is likely to be low quality but first on the market? “ It is true that many companies trie to keep these levels of risk and low quality secret. But this behavior is viewed as unprofessional. Doctors perform operations which pose different degrees of threat to their patients but they must relveal the level of risk and secure informed consent.

“This was the point of 1.14. Promote maximum quality and minimum cost to the employer, the client, the user and the public. Make any tradeoffs clear to all parties concerned.” We do understand that in any software development there will be trade-offs; but the professional is obliged to make these clear.

The next two points are straightforward to fix. Thank you. Point 4 is right on the mark. 6.15 is in need of clarification sharing "useful software?related knowledge, inventions, or discoveries with the profession". A qualification is needed respecting rights of the third parties and confidentality. We are also adding a caveat about not violating or causing loss of trade secrets, competitive dis advantage etc. This will also cross reference 4.05

Point 5 asks in regard to 1.15 “Are there reasonable and sufficient industry standards that apply to most or all areas of software engineering? “ We struggled with this. We know there are different standards for data processing and real time systems. Unfortunately some developers generalize from the existence of different standards to “there are NO standards. We tried to cover this with the word ‘ appropriate’ in the introduction to this section. One of the things that make standards appropriate is the domain of application, shrinkwrap software etc.

There is also the problem that there may be no best standard for a particular domain. “ Work to follow industry standards that are most appropriate for the task at hand, departing from these only when technically justified.” will change to “Work to follow SUFFICIENT industry standards WHEN AVAILABLE that are most appropriate for the task at hand,...” We hope that “sufficient” implies that there are several acceptable methodologies and that “when available” allows for the fact that for certain projects there may be no best method.

Point 6 refers to the conflict of interest clause in 3.07

You are correct in that we did intend the code to be read as whole and in the preamble suggested that individual items be read in the context of the code as a whole. We want to make clear We have decided to insert cross reference clauses where it is needed, rather than intreoduce more repetition into the Code.

. Your concern with the absolute prohibitions of 3.07 was shared by others who commeted on the code. The intent of the conflict of interest clause was that potential conflicts of interest be revealed and addressed in ways acceptable to the customer. So we have deleted 3.07 and modified 3.06 in the following way.

3.06 Disclose to all concerned parties those potential conflicts of interest that cannot reasonably be avoided or escaped and aspire to resolve them.

NEED INFORMED CONSENT WORD HERE I favor avoiding undisclosed potential conflicts. At the same time, expect professional behavior. [1i]I may participate. However, I must disclose the conflicts before participation and clearly state

Perhaps we might include soemthing about divulging interests and seeking agreement from affected parties that this is ok prior to the decision making. If not then they have to cease their involvement. I think it is a case of transparency. The verb "refuse" is too strong. Also, restricting the statement to "financial interest" likely misses other important "interests." A Software Engineer with an interest should fully disclose the nature of the interest. Based on the Software Engineer's belief that an unbiased opinion can be rendered and the comfort of the decision?making body a decision regarding the nature of the participation of the Software Engineer in the decision process can be made and acted on accordingly. [10i] But 3.07 is simply too simplistic, in my opinion.
