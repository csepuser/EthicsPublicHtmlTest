Re. SESSION TITLE:

"Ethical Issues and Professionalism in Computer Science and Software Engineering"

PROPOSED SPEAKERS:

o "Ethical Complexities in Computer Science and Software Engineering," Dr. Michael McFarland, Boston College, mcfarlan@bcuxs2.bc.edu McFarland's paper introduces and identifies ethical issues relevant to Computer Science and Software Engineering. The ineffectiveness of professional codes of ethics to tangibly improve ethical conduct is discussed. The relevant experiences of other professions are addressed.

o "A Justification of Professional Responsibility" Dr. Donald Gotterbarn, East Tennessee State University, d.gotterbarn@computer.org Gotterbarn explores the ethical responsibilities of Computer Scientists and Software Engineers. Issues both common and distinct amongst safety and mission critical, and office and recreational environments are considered.

o "Ethical Negotiations," Robert J. Melford, R.J. Melford & Associates, r.melford@ieee.org Melford discusses conflict resolution and multilateral ethical responsibilities amongst society, the profession, employers, and the individual. The roles of consensus, trust, power, and persuasion are considered.

The section concludes with the panel discussion:

o "Recent Responses to Ethical Issues," ? Dr. Mario R. Barbacci, Software Engineering Institute, barbacci@sei.cmu.edu, (Moderator) ? Dr. Patricia Douglas, IBM Skill Dynamics (proposed) ? Dr. Doris Carver, Louisiana State University (proposed) ? Prof. John Werth, University of Texas (proposed) Barbacci's panel provides a critical analysis of the preceding papers within the context of the industrial and educational communities' experience with computing related ethical factors. McFarland, Gotterbarn, and Melford join the panel for an open discussion with the audience.

SYNOPSIS:

Computing professionals have been viewed as considering their discipline as hard, compared to ethics, which is considered soft. Computing is felt to be deterministic, while ethics etherial. Some computing professionals consider ethical matters to generally fall outside of their sphere of competency, diminishing their ability and responsibility to act.

Yet technology does not exist in a vacuum. Computing technology is now integral to function of the global economy. This has insured that computing has rapidly become an inseparable component of the human experience. The developing Global Information Infrastructure (GII) merely accelerates his process. Ethical conflict is an inescapable factor in the human experience. Ethical conflict and computing are likewise linked.

What ethical factors do computing related research, design, and development activities introduce? Are these ethical issues new, or have other disciplines already dealt with them? Are Computer Scientists and Software Engineers solely responsible for identifying and resolving ethical conflicts? With computing's transcendence of national borders, can cultural issues be managed in this dawning age of the GII?

With these factors and questions in mind, this session assesses the ability of the computing profession to address the identification and resolution of ethical conflicts. The speakers and panelists consider the experiences of the professional, industrial, research, and educational communities in furthering ethical responsiveness.
