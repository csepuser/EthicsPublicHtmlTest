Enforcement

I am glad that the Code of Ethics is being revisited, and its scope expanded beyond the ACM. This is, I know, difficult and often thankless work, but it's necessary if organizations like ACM and IEEE/CS are to become true governing bodies of the profession. The alternative ?? governmental certification and licensing ?? is far worse, and I'm convinced that the time is fast approaching when we will have to demonstrate the ability to govern ourselves or be subjected to the heavy hand of Big Brother. That said, I have deep-seated reservations about several provisions of the current Draft, and I hope that the Executive Committee contemplates a complete rework after comments are re?ceived. In its current state, this Draft is nowhere near ready for release. Perhaps my most fundamental reservation has to do with the fact that no enforcement mechanisms are prescribed. As the Pre?amble suggests, ethics is not an exact science; it's a subjective matter, and no enumeration of "principles" can transform it into an objective discipline. Codes of ethics are not statements on the nature of abstract "right" and "wrong"; rather, they are statements of feelings shared by certain individuals at certain points in time. This Draft attempts to describe how members of ACM and IEEE/CS feel about professional standards in the year 1997. The reason for making it a public document is to give laypersons some idea of what those feelings are, and thus of what they can expect when dealing with a professional in the field of software engineering. If a code of ethics is to have any meaning at all, it must offer remedies to people who believe it has been violated, and the only appropriate way to claim such remedies is to appeal to the authority which promulgated the code. In this case, such an appeal would be made to ACM, IEEE/CS, or another professional society subscribing to the code. The disciplinary tools at the disposal of such societies range from warnings and reprimands to suspension or expulsion.

Organizations use these tools, and subscribe to codes of ethics, because they seek to assure the public that their members will act in certain ways, and that those who do not will lose their standing as members. Such assurances are the alternative to governmental regulation. At the very minimum, therefore, the code must require of subscribing organizations that they maintain a compliance body charged with receiving and evaluating complaints against their members. To ensure fairness, basic procedural safeguards should also be outlined.

For the sake of discussion, let me offer the proposal of inserting a new Principle, "Governing Bodies," between existing Principles 7 and 8. The inclusion of such a Principle would make it clear that we, as software professionals, consider ourselves uniquely qualified to govern our own, and that we are asserting our intention to do so. The new Principle would deal with the relationship of subscribing organizations to the code, and of members to their governing organizations. It would set forth the basic obligations on both sides, including the obligation of professional organizations to evaluate complaints against their members and the right of members to be judged by impartial peers.

I realize that addressing these matters will necessitate a substantial amount of rework, and I deeply regret it. However, by the terms of its own charter, the Committee really cannot avoid dealing with the issue of enforcement. The original joint steering committee was charged with establishing "appropriate set(s) of standards for professional practice of software engineering upon which industrial decisions, professional certification, and educational curricula can be based." Certification presupposes some means of enforcement. Speaking to you as colleagues and fellow professionals, I assure you that failure to make provision for enforcement will result in a code which is essentially irrelevant and universally perceived as such. Consequently, it will do nothing to avert, and may even invite, external regulation of a sort none of us wants.

It will not do to say that enforcement provisions can be left to the subscribing organizations. If our goal is to formulate standards of professional conduct applicable to the entire range of software practice ?? and that should be our goal ?? we must insist on a uniform interpretation of those standards. If we do not, the code will be interpreted by the various organizations in ways which diverge over time, eventually resulting in a muddle of conflicting precedents. For this reason, my own preference would be to remove complaint proceedings from the jurisdiction of the subscribing organizations by requiring a multi?organizational composition for all panels of inquiry. Of course, the code of ethics itself need not delve into such procedural details, but it must set forth the general guidelines for its own enforcement. [55a]

Sexist The authors of the Draft have attempted to avoid the generic masculine by resorting to the plural. Now, I dislike the generic masculine as much as anyone, not so much because it's politically incorrect as because it's inaccurate. However, I've found that the best substitute is the second person singular, which has the advantages of being simple, accurate, and direct. "Software engineers shall ensure that they are qualified..." sounds sterile and remote, as if it applies to someone else. "As a software engineer, you shall ensure..." commands much more attention. I recommend recasting the entire document in the second person.[55a]

I'm sure you'll agree that we do not want to discourage the prompt identification of incompetent practitioners, or practitioners operating outside their field of competence. Nobody is in a better position to do this than colleagues – indeed, they do so now, as a matter of common (if informal) practice. At the very least, therefore, 7.07 should be amended to read something like this:

Not interfere in the professional career progression of any colleague, except in cases where concern for client or public welfare causes a professional to question the competence of a colleague for the work being performed.[55a]
