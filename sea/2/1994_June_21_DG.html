"COMPUTING ETHICS: WORLD'S TWO LARGEST COMPUTER PROFESSIONAL SOCIETIES JOIN FORCES IN INTERNATIONAL PROJECT"

Washington, DC, Rayburn House Office Building, Congressional Briefing, organized by Representatives George Brown (D-CA) and Edward Markey (D-MA), (June 21, 1994) ?? The world's two largest organizations of computing professionals have mounted a joint effort to establish internationally recognized standards for the professional and ethical conduct of software engineers. The one hundred thousand member (world-wide), Washington, DC based IEEE Computer Society (IEEE-CS), and the seventy thousand member (world-wide), New York, NY based Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) recently established their joint task force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices (SEEPP) to "codify and document the software engineering profession's collective wisdom for resolving ethical conflicts," said Robert J. Melford, Computer Society Co?chair for the task force.

ACM Co-chair, Dr. Donald Gotterbarn added that "this task force will focus both on 'Ethics' and 'Standards of Practice.' The latter providing guidelines and recommended practices to help the computing professional meet their professional obligations to quality and higher care in difficult situations, Both Co-chairs went on to emphasize that "[software engineering] as a profession whose activities are trans-national, the SEEPP task force must also address cultural, organizational, and national characteristics that bear on the definition of Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices."

The SEEPP task force is approaching the one?third mark of an aggressive eighteen month schedule to complete it's work. It's work force has entered a growth phase as computing professionals have begun volunteering their labor.

Melford and Gotterbarn explained that the IEEE Computer Society's long history of developing and establishing internationally recognized standards for the design, test, construction, and operation of computer hardware and software, in conjunction with its standards covering the management of software engineering projects give the SEEPP task force "a recognized methodology for developing and documenting the profession's consensus, in an international venue." The task force's participants have increased as it finalizes the organization of a set of "Standards Working Groups," which are tasked with addressing specific areas of interest and concern.

Melford and Gotterbarn stated that when the SEEPP task force first convened, at the end of April, initially seven different areas for Working Groups were identified. These areas of interest include privacy, security, testing and reliability, competence, duties to society, and intellectual property. A seventh area was identified for "ethical tools," they said. Gotterbarn explained that these might include such things as how to establish and operate a "Hot Line" where software engineers could query recognized experts about ethical problems they were encountered.

Both Chairmen also noted that there probably are more then these seven areas of concern to the computing profession. They stated that the task force could establish additional Working Groups if there was sufficient volunteer interest in the profession to ensure that once work had begun, it could be sustained until completion.

The SEEPP task force is part of a larger project, the IEEE-CS/ACM Steering Committee for the Establishment of Software Engineering as a Profession. The Steering Committee has also established two other task forces to review academic curriculum and define the body of knowledge. Dr. Mario Barbacci and Prof. Stuart Zweben are Chairman and Vice?Chairman, respectively.
