I am responding to the code of ethics. Overall, the code covers most of the areas concerning Software Engineers. I have a few questions on the code. Under PRINCIPLES v 2.1 the statement "free of error" appears. Is there really any large software package that can be "free of error?" If so, I have not found it yet.

The other comment I have concerns what to do if problems arise. I know this code does not cover all possible scenarios, but problems are inevidable to occur during large projects. One solution may be to recursively use the code until the project is done.

Overall, good job. Codes of this nature are difficult to create and publish.

Thank you for your time,

Jason Nikolai
Software Engineering Student
University of Wisconsin - Eau Claire

COMMENTS ON THE SOFTWARE ENGINEERING ETHICS CODE

INTRODUCTION

The introduction agreeably establishes that the Software Engineering Ethics Code is a joint effort between the IEEE computer Society and ACM council members. It is appropriate that the individual authors that made up each of these groups is specified at the end of the Software Engineering Ethics Code. The third paragraph mentions several computing and engineering codes, which were used as references in this document. However, no where in this document was there reference to the specific parenthetically documented sources. Also in interest of these sources is the implicit question of which ethical theories are these based. For example, were these sources based on Kantian or Utilitarian principles.

PREAMBLE v 2.1

Paragraph III: There are three levels of ethical obligations that are listed in paragraph three and then described in later paragraphs. However, there is a deficit of pragmatic examples that could potentially identify and illustrate specific ethical obligations. In other words, it might be easier for the reader to relate to these generalizations if the reader could identify with them via the use of real life computer related scenarios.

PRINCIPLES v 2.1

The lists of principles which are associated with keywords that are perhaps too extensive in number. Maybe some of these listed ideas would best be presented in paragraph format with the intent of enticing the readers desire mentally process this information. Each of the listed ideas could be elaborated upon so that examples might be included as part of the code.

Dear Mr. Gotterbarn,

I am a senior undergraduate student here at the University of Wisconsin--Eau Claire. I am currently enrolled in Dr. David Nuesse's Ethics in Computer Science class. Yesterday Dr. Nuesse emailed our class a copy of the Software Engineering Ethics Code that you helped develop. He then asked us to provide some feedback to you on this Code of Ethics.

Let me begin by saying that I am very impressed by the detail and content of this Code. I feel that it is very complete and definitely an improvement upon the ACM Code of Ethics, which I feel at times is too general. One feature of the Software Engineering Ethics Code that I especially like is the different responsibilities and obligations that the software engineer has in the different roles that he/she may play.

I think that my biggest complaint about the Software Engineering Ethics Code is that I do not feel it stresses that a software engineer should always work to achieve the highest quality possible in his/her work. The ACM Code of Ethics is very clear about this. I feel that trying to achieve perfection is very important and to accept less is unethical. This is why I feel that it is important that it be stressed that a software engineer needs to attempt to work at the highest level and not settle for anything less.

The other complaint that I have involves a topic covered in Dr. Nuesse's Ethics in Computer Science class. In his class, the issue of the right of privacy an employee has concerning his/her email would come up repeatedly. I was hoping that this Code you helped to develop would specifically address this issue and under what situations it is ethical or unethical. I guess I am especially concerned with this topic as in December I will be graduating and will be getting a job with a company where I perhaps will not have the privacy I seemingly have with my email right now. I know the Code states in Principle 5 (MANAGEMENT) that management is to "assure that employees are informed of standards before being held to them (5.01)" and "assure employees know the employer's policies and procedures for protecting passwords, files, and other confidential information (5.02)". These two principles do address this topic somewhat, but I guess I was hoping for a more explicit policy towards email in the work place.

Now that I have shared my complaints about the Software Engineering Ethics Code with you, let me also share what I like best about the code. First of all, I like the idea expressed in the last set of principles that a software engineer needs to maintain competence. I feel that an education does not end with a degree and with a field involving the fast-changing world of computers, it is important to stay informed.

Secondly, I like the principles that you have developed about a software engineer's responsibilities to his profession. I feel that it is important to, as is stated in principle 6.13, "share useful software-related knowledge, inventions, or discoveries with the profession, for example, by presenting papers at professional meetings, by publishing articles in the technical press, and by serving on the profession's standard-setting bodies." I also like the principle about a software engineer taking an active role in establishing an organizational environment that promotes ethical behavior.

Finally, I was glad to see some other concepts included in this Code that are missing from the ACM Code of Ethics. These include an emphasis on documentation and testing of software, and having qualified people work on a project. Other concepts I liked were that software engineers should warn the public about the dangers and risks associated with computers, and that management should not ask its employees to do something inconsistent with the Software Engineering Ethics Code.

In conclusion, I was very impressed with the Code you developed, and I hope that it is seen by many people and becomes an industry standard.

Thank you,

Steve Wall

Don and Simon,

Congratulations on your good work on the SE Code of Ethics. I liked the organization and the content.

There is, however, one large omission. Those of us who do "human factors" research (e.g., user interface evaluation) often use human subjects in our tests. There should be a section in the code of ethics dealing with this specific area. Presumably, it would cover the standard issues in a fashion similar to medical ethics: informed consent, rights of the human subject, responsibilities of the experimenter, and confidentiality. It would also need to cover special issues like non-disclosure.

If you like, I can take a crack at drafting a set of guidelines.

Walt
