> You have asked ACM SIGSOFT for its comments on the draft Code of

>
> In any case, I have some of my own comments, which I include below.
> And, because some of my concerns are (to me at least) quite serious,
> let me take this opportunity to state clearly that until some of these
> problems are clarified and/or fixed, that ACM SIGSOFT does *not*
> endorse this draft code.
>
> In general, I think that my comments fall into two categories:
>
> *General comments about ethics (such as financial conflict),
> where I may be especially naive.
>
> *Comments about places where I think the code is too naive
> about some special properties of software and the software
> industry.
>
> I'm less worried about the first category than the second because it
> would, if I am correct, significantly reduce the collection of people
> that might pay attention to and learn from the proposed code.
>
> Feel free to pass these comments around as you wish (preferably
> including this message as context). I suspect that they sound far
> more negative than they should, but perhaps they will generate some
> useful improvements.
>
> David
> *****
> I've just read the draft of the software engineering code of ethics.
> And, indeed, I have a couple of comments.
>
> 1) There is not a single mention of the issue of modifying or
> changing
> existing software. Since this is a significant piece of the industry,
> the omission is obvious and serious. I don't believe that adding in a
> few words can handle this easily, either, because in many cases the
> pressures to make changes without a "full" understanding of the
> software are real and are reasonable.
>
[Miller, Keith] It is "reasonable" to make changes in code
without a full understanding of the software??? That doesn't sound
reasonable or ethical to me. He's right... we have a serious
disagreement here.

[sr] Perhaps the issue is to undertake appropriate impact analysis prior
to software modification taking into account the role/functioality of the
software ? the greater thescopr wrt people, organisations, society the
more care required. My ethical hotspot concept!!

> Another take on this is that the ability to change and evolve software
> is one of the primary characteristics that distinguishes software from
> other engineering disciplines. If we don't account for the
> distinguishing characteristics of software, then we can use "any old
> engineering ethics code."
>
> [ dg? I AGREE THAT MODIFYABILITY IS ONE OF THE DISTINGUISHING
> CHARATERISTICS BUT WHEN WE TEACH MAINTENANCE WE SAY THAT THE NORMAL
> DEVELOPMENT PROCESS IS APPLIED TO AN EXISTING SOFTWARE ARTIFACT?
> UNDERSTAND THE NEEDED CHANGE, DESIGN THE CHANGE, IMPELEMENT AND TEST
> THE
> CHANGE AND MAKE SURE THAT THE CHANGE HAD NO UNEXPECTED SIDE EFFECTS.]
>
> 2) My reading is that the code implicitly focuses on contract
> software,
> where specifications and design documents play a critical and direct
> role.
> However, a significant amount of the industry now works in the
> shrinkwrap
> world, where these issues are wildly different. Yes, specification
> exists. Yes, design exists. But phrases like "satisfy the user's
> requirements", "have the client's approval", "understand fully the
> specifications for software on which they work", etc., are at best
> difficult to interpret and are at worst meaningless.
> [DG. wE NEED TO ADDRESS THIS? BUT i WOULD HATE TO HAVE TO ADD A
> CAVEAT
> TO EVERY STATEMENT SAYING, "IN THOSE CASES WHERE THERE WAS A CLIENT".
> iS
> THERE A MORE GENERAL WAY TO DO THIS?PERHAPS AT THE BEGINNING WE COULD
> RECOGNIZE THIS DEVELOPMET METHOD AND REINTERPRET CLIENT TO BE THE
> COMPANY
> PRODUCING THE VENTURE PRODUCT ETC. tHE CODE IS BEGINNING TO LOOK LIKE
> A
> BOOK.]
>
[Miller, Keith] This is a serious problem. The client, in this
case, can be marketing of the company producing the software. Or the
developer him/herself becomes the client. I think we should stipulate a
"client" for shinkwrapped software: the person or persons who must be
satisfied before the product is shipped.

[sr] Yes this is tricky! Keith's idea points towards philanthropy. What
if the shrinkwrapped client agrees software that is damaging is some way
to some users in the community ? where does their input come in. I think
its a new twist on "end user" issues ? We need to address this point.
Good one Notkin!!!
> 3) 1.06 talks about "effective procedures for promotion of quality
> and
> reduction of risk." Seems reasonable, for sure. But what about a
> situation where a company (quite reasonably) chooses a niche in which
> their immediate goal is to produce a software product that is likely
> to be
> low quality but first on the market? Or what about a company that
> intentionally takes a high?risk strategy (perhaps, for example,
> depending
> on other products that aren't yet completed)? Perhaps situations like
> these are covered indirectly in the draft code, but I don't believe
> that
> this is sufficient.
> [DG ? I DON'T KNOW WHAT TO DO HERE. IF HE IS TALKING ABOUT DECIEVING
> THE
> PUBLIC, THEN IT IS NOT ACCEPTABLE IN A CODE OF ETHICS. IF THERE A
> REASONABLE WAY TO FIT INFORMED CONSENT HERE?]
>
[Miller, Keith] Risks are unavoidable and acceptable, but ONLY
when the affected parties are able to choose that risk. Disclaimers on
such software would be required. Contractual bargains must be explicit.

[sr] I agree with Keith

> 4) 1.14 talks about making tradeoffs visible to all parties concerned,
> including the public. And 6.13 talks about sharing "useful
> software?related knowledge, inventions, or discoveries with the
> profession". If the draft code doesn't make it clear that companies
> and software engineers have interests???in particular, certain classes
> of intellectual property???that needn't be disclosed, or that must be
> disclosed with care, then it will not be of use to many software
> engineering practitioners. You address this somewhat in Principle 4,
> especially 4.05, but explicitly observing this inherent set of
> conflicts seems of value.
> [ DG ANY SUGGESTIONS FOR REWORDING?]
>
[Miller, Keith] This seems doable with a few extra words. I
trust it to your pen, Don.

[sr] guess we can fix this in Dec!!

> 5) 1.15 talks about departing from appropriate industry standards
> "only when technically justified." How about economically
> justifications? Even more critical, are there reasonable and
> sufficient industry standards that apply to most or all areas of
> software engineering? Safety?critical software? Real?time embedded
> software? Shrinkwrap software? Operating system software?
> Transaction processing software? Game and entertainment software?
> Internet?based software? Etc. If not, is it "ethical" to provide a
> principle that is unsatisfiable?
> [DG I THINK HE MAY HAVE MISSED THE WORD 'APPROPRIATE'. ONE OF THE
> THINGS THAT MAKE STANDARDS APPROPRIATE IS THE DOMAIN OF APPLICATION,
> SHRINKWRAP SOFTWARE ETC.]
>
[Miller, Keith] "Sufficient industry standards WHEN AVAILABLE"
would make it more accurate.

[sr] Yes I agree

> 6) 3.07 discusses avoiding financial conflicts of interest. Well, I'm
> no expert here, but let me give you two examples where such conflict
> would exist according to 3.07, but where it seems ludicrous.
>
> a) I was once on a program committee for a software
> engineering conference. I was advocating the acceptance of
> a videotape by a person at a company for a presentation.
> I was asked by another committee member if I had stock in
> the company. Well, it turns out I did (I'd guess it was worth
> under $1500 at the time), although it hadn't occurred to me.
> Was I unethical? Nope. Was there an appearance of being
> unethical? Well, perhaps. Would the conference (and the
> profession) have been better had I recused myself? Surely
> not. Indeed, given that almost everyone in the community owns
> mutual funds that own high?tech stocks, I'll wager that this
> kind of broad "financial interest" conflict would make it all
> but impossible to make progress in many bodies.
>
> (Professional standards committees would, I suspect, fall
> apart entirely. Why? Because all industrial members would,
> according to 3.07, have an ethical conflict if their employers
> would benefit from their involvement???and this is, of course,
> in some sense the primary goal of sending employees to serve
> on standardization boards.)
>
> b) The State of Washington has a new and extremely firm ethics
> code. Hunter Simpson was a member of the state's Information
> System's Board (ISB), a gubenatorial appointee. He's been CEO
> of a company or two and is quite wealthy. He owned $9K of IBM
> stock. He was involved in a (unanimous) decision to award a
> state contract to IBM. He was ultimately punished by the
> State Ethics Board for having a conflict of interest. This
> was (although you might not be able to tell from my brief
> description) crazy as can be. If applied even more
> aggressively (easy to believe in our state's case) you could
> imagine situations in which the only people who could sit on
> the state's ISB are people who have no assets and have no
> knowledge of technology.
>
> OK, I'm sure that these are not what you had in mind. And I suspect
> (again) that these kind of "exceptions" are covered elsewhere in the
> draft. (And I'm sure you have way better examples than mine, since I
> am confident you've thought about this.) But 3.07 is simply too
> simplistic, in my opinion.
> [DG? HOW DO WE HANDLE THIS? hE KNOWS HE SHOULD NOT READ THE SINGLE
> BULLET
> AND THAT IT IS HANDLED IN IN THER SECTIONS OF 3, BUT THEN INSISTS ON
> READING 3.07 AS A STAND ALONE ITEM.]
>
[Miller, Keith] I think we have a real problem here. He shrugs
off cases that, IMHO, are pretty clearly cases where there IS a
appearance of conflict of interest. In the case of mutual funds, I can
understand the problem he has. But if a state official has major
holdings in a computer company that is doing business with the state,
than the official had better put that stuff in a blind trust... whether
this guy likes it or not! I think the code has struck a nerve with this
guy, and that nerves deserves to be stung.

[sr] Positions of trus and authority requiure the highest ethical
standards. Perhaps we might include soemthing about divulging interests
and seeking agreement from affected parties that this is ok prior to the
decision making. If not then they have to cease their involvement. I
think it is a case of transparency.


> 7) 6.07 says to "only accept remuneration appropriate to professional
> qualifications or experience." Forbes (I think) just printed its list
> of the top 100 richest people in technology. Many of them got rich
> essentially as software engineers who started companies. I'd hate to
> be in the middle of a discussion about whether they are accepted
> appropriate remuneration.
> [DG POINTING TO THE EXISTENCE OF ROBBER BARONS TO SHOW THAT AN
> IMPERATIVE
> DOESN'T BELONG IN THE CODE DOES NOT SEEM REASONABLE. HOWEVER,
> PERSONALLY,
> I HAVE ALWAYS HAD TROUBLE WITH THIS CLAUSE. WHEN I CONSULT, MY PRICE
> IS
> IN PART BASED ON THE CRITICALITY OF THE PROJECT AND WHICH ELEMENTS OF
> MY
> TALENTS ARE EMPLOYED. i DO ACCOUNTING PACKAGES AT A CHEAP RATE, BUT
> NUCLEAR REACTOR DEVELOPMENT IS AT A HIGHER RATE. Same guy, but
> different
> talents are required. Comments? ]
>
>
[Miller, Keith] Maybe we should make this more explicit: "never
take inflated wages when you know it is a bribe."


[sr] I am reminded of my time in Poland when my Polish colleagues could not afford latest proprietory software because of global pricing policies form MS and the like designed to maximise profit and minimise administrative effort regardless of social/economic impact in developing
countries. This is a difficult clause but I don't think its just about bribes it is about social justice. A code should set teh moral high ground for us to aspire to. Somehow we have to write a clause that does this but takes the RB supporters with us. December's "toughie" task!!!
