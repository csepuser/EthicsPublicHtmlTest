Professionalization of Software Engineering and Executive Committees of the ACM and the IEEE-CS Professionalization of Software Engineering and Executive Committees of the ACM and the IEEE-CS - December 16, 1997 (Level 2) E-mail

December 16, 1997

To the Joint IEEE-CS/ACM Steering Committee on the Professionalization of Software Engineering and Executive Committees of the ACM and the IEEE-CS:

The Task Force on Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practices has completed its work in developing a Software Engineering Code of Ethics and Professional Practice and recommends its approval by both professional societies. We recommend this action for the following reasons: Support by both societies will be a public assertion by the profession of a unified commitment to ethical responsibility, The Code represents an international standard of practice, The Code has been reviewed and adopted by industry, The Code presents a broad consensus of the profession, and There is wide demonstrated positive support, including from Academia, for the Code.

This Code complements the more general codes of ethics of both societies which address the issues of the computing profession as a whole. The Code of Software Engineering Ethics and Professional Practice addresses the ethical issues and standards of conduct which specifically arise within the practice of software engineering. In this sense the code is not a replacement for any existing code.

The approval of this Code by both societies will be a significant forward step for the software engineering profession. The cooperative support given by both societies to the development and approval of this Code underscores the importance of a commitment to ethical practices by the profession and will serve as a model for other developing professions.

The development of this Code was unique in its international flavor. The task force and the executive committee had international representation, including from Europe, Africa, Australia, and North America. As the process of development and revision proceeded, this network of international participation and comment spread throughout the world. In an effort to be consistent with the codes of the major computing professional societies throughout the world, the task force solicited responses from members of the major computing societies. Comment and support was also received from the International Federation of Information Processing (IFIP). The task force also included two lawyers specializing in computer law, and one representative from the military.

The Code has been positively reviewed by several ethics officers and ethics directors of multinational organizations after sharing it with software engineers in their organizations. Such industries included: Lockheed Martin, Northrup Gruman, and Digital Equipment. We also heard from CEO's of smaller companies indicating strong support for the Code. In fact, the Code has already, in its preliminary form, been adapted by St.Paul Companies as their information systems code of ethics with only minor modifications, such as, replacing the expression "software engineer" with "IS professional".

The Code was developed using a process of continuous review and revision. The final draft of the Code, together with a questionaire, was published on several web sites, in some ACM SIG journals and in Computer and CACM. The results of the survey were that most items had greater than 95 percent support with many at 100 percent. The highest level of opposition to any clause was 12%. In the light of feedback, the Principles and associated Clauses have been reviewed and modified where necessary. We are confident that if the survey were to be taken again, all Clauses would receive an approval rating of over 95% The Code has also received a positive response from academe. We have received several requests to have the Code included in computer ethics and software engineering textbooks and have been asked to develop case studies for software engineering classes based on the Code.

The Code has already proven to be a dynamic and useful document becoming a model within at least one industry. Both societies have already recognized that education about their codes of ethics is an important issue. Several members of the SEEPP task force are on the IEEE-CS and the ACM committees which are responsible for the maintenance of their codes and education of the membership about their codes of ethics. To support the maintenance of and education about the Code, we plan to maintain it initially on the Software Engineering Ethics Research Institute's and Centre for Computing and Social Responsibility's web sites. The SEEPP Task Force will continue to gather input from the community about the Code and develop and share examples of using the Code. Industry has already donated case studies related to the Clauses of the Code. It would be beneficial to the profession if both societies endorsed this effort and approved the Code.

Sincerely, The Executive Commitee of the SEEP Task Force
