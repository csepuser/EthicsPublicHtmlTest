Interviewer: Michael Davis
Interviewee: John Weckert
Date Approved: August 9, 2001
Location : Tran Station, Wagga Wagga NSW Australia

Interview of John Weckert

1. What is your educational background?

JW: (Summary) Undergraduate major in history and later a Diploma in Computer Science, MA and PhD in Philosophy.

2. What sort of organization do you work for? What do you do there?

JW: (Summary) University. Teach in the Information Technology Discipline—Primarily technical issues such as Artificial Intelligence, Data Communications, and Computer Ethics

3. What experience, if any, have you had in software development? If you are a software developer, what led you to that field?

JW: None. I do not consider software development as a strength of mine...Oh, I was involved in the development of a decision support system for vitraculture program that is being sold professionally.

4. Are you an engineer?

JW: I am not an engineer.

5. How did you hear about the IEEE/ACM Joint Task force on Software Engineering and Professional Practice 9SEEPP)?

JW: I Met Don [Gotterbarn] at a meeting [1994?]. He told me about it. Work had already been going on for some time.

6. What lead you to participate in SEEPP’s work?

[No answer]

7. Were you familiar with codes of ethics before you became involved in SEEPP? Explain.

JW: I am familiar with the ACS Code. I have only looked at codes in the computing area. My knowledge of them is gotten primarily from teaching and a little from work with ACS.

8. In what ways did you participate in SEEPP’s Work, especially in the process of preparing the Code?

JW: I got in late. I remember commenting on one of the drafts [version 3?]. I do not know if people took notice. It is hard to keep enthusiasm when physically removed from the project.

9. By what means did you participate? For example, did you participate by email, or phone, or though face-to-face meetings, or by letter, or by informal conversation, or the like?

JW: Email.

10. Did any of these means of participation seem to work better than the others? Any seem to work worse? Which would you recommend as best? Why?

JW: I can only answer hypothetically because I only worked through email. I think face-to-face works better. You accomplish more in one hour face-to-face then in many hours of email.”

JW:(Not response to question, but a comment) The Code is primarily a US document. It started off as an ACM-IEEE document. The IEEE says it’s an international organization but it always seemed American to me. Don [Gotterbarn] is now working to make the Code international.


11. Any events that particularly stick out in your mind relevant to the process? (The more detail the better)

JW: I have a very vague recollection of strong disagreements. I do not remember what they were about. I only read about them in email.


12. What comments did you make?

JW: Maybe about accountability and responsibility.

13. Has your thinking about codes of ethics changed as a result of your participation in SEEPP’s work? How?

JW: I think about the roles of codes now. Not 100% clear why we should bother having them? I think more about professions in the software development (maybe more about it since I know Don).

14. What, in your opinion, is important about having a code of ethics?

(Summary)-What a professional body claims is important; gives profession credibility. Teaching Code gives you something to hang your hat on. With a code, it is not just an academic making claims. The code could be used in licensing. One person was kicked out of the ACS for Code violations; codes influence behavior of those in the profession. A code is a guide to behavior.

15. Is there anything about your participation that you are especially please or unhappy about?

JW: (Summary) Pleased-nothing special. Disappointed about amount of own participation.

16. Is there anything about the final code that you are especially pleased with or unhappy about?

JW: (Summary) Unhappy? Nothing. Happy? I like this code better then the ACS Code. The ACS Code only has short sections. The SE code is detailed, more focused. Upfront it mentions groups. But I have not looked closely at it recently.

17. Is there anyone whose participation in the process seems to you especially important? Explain

JW: Don-maybe you Michael [Davis] I don’t know- Simon [Rogerson].

18. Anyone who you think we should be sure to talk to? Explain.

JW: To tell you more about the Code? [John Weckert did not have a list of which interviewers were already going to talk to. - DG] From the ACS [Australian Computer Society], talk with John Hughes, John Ridge the president, and Henry Maynard. In Melbourne. These are IT folks. Andrew Alexandria [Philosophy department, University of Melbourne] knows a lot about codes.

19. If you had been in charge of the process, what, if anything, would you have done differently?

JW: That is a hard question to answer. My participation was not great. In retrospect, I think it would have been good to have had more formal involvement of other societies early on, not just the involvement of people who were in those societies [IEEE-CS and ACM]

20. Is there anything we should have asked but didn’t? Anything to add to what you have already said?

JW: Can’t think of anything.
